# DAMP — Drug and Alcohol Management Plan

The Drug and Alcohol Management Plan (DAMP) is a regulatory requirement for certain aviation industry participants. It establishes obligations around drug and alcohol testing, education, and management for people who perform safety-sensitive aviation activities (SSAAs).

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** CASR Part 99 — Drug and alcohol management plans and testing. Civil Aviation Order 82.0 — Drug and alcohol testing.

---

## What is a SSAA?

A Safety-Sensitive Aviation Activity (SSAA) is any activity that, if performed carelessly or incompetently, could endanger the life of a person or the safety of an aircraft. For airport operations, SSAAs include:

- Conducting aerodrome inspections
- Operating vehicles on the movement area
- Supervising airside works
- Providing aerodrome information to aircraft

As a FOO/ARO/WSO, you are performing SSAAs and are subject to the DAMP.

---

## DAMP obligations

The operator (your employer) must have a DAMP that covers:

- **Random testing** — employees performing SSAAs may be randomly selected for drug and alcohol testing at any time, without notice
- **Pre-employment testing** — testing before commencing a role that involves SSAAs
- **Post-incident testing** — testing after a significant incident or accident
- **Reasonable cause testing** — testing when there is reason to believe an employee may be impaired
- **Return-to-duty testing** — testing after a period of suspension or treatment for substance use

---

## Fitness for duty

The fundamental obligation is straightforward: you must be fit for duty when performing SSAAs. This means:

- Blood alcohol content (BAC) below 0.02% (lower than the standard 0.05% road limit)
- Not impaired by any drug — prescription or otherwise — to the extent that your performance of SSAAs is affected

Some prescription medications can impair performance even at therapeutic doses. If you are taking any medication — prescription or over-the-counter — that could affect your alertness, coordination, or judgment, consult your doctor about whether it is safe to perform SSAAs while taking it. If in doubt, do not perform SSAAs and inform your supervisor.

---

## Your obligations

As an employee performing SSAAs, you must:

- Comply with drug and alcohol testing when required
- Not perform SSAAs when impaired
- Report to your employer if you are taking medication that may affect your performance
- Cooperate with the DAMP process

A refusal to submit to a required test is treated the same as a positive test result.

---

## What happens if you test positive

A positive test result or refusal to test triggers a defined management process under the DAMP. The specific steps depend on the employer's DAMP, but typically involve:

- Immediate removal from SSAAs
- Management counselling
- Assessment and support options
- Return-to-duty requirements including follow-up testing

CASA can also take regulatory action, including suspension or cancellation of licences or certificates, following a positive test.

---

## Supporting a colleague

If you observe a colleague who appears impaired while performing or about to perform SSAAs, you have both a professional and a regulatory obligation to act. This is not a comfortable situation — but the alternative is allowing an impaired person to make decisions that could endanger aircraft and passengers.

Report your concern to your supervisor. You do not need to make a diagnosis — you only need to report what you observed. Let the DAMP process determine the next steps.

---

## DAMP and the broader safety culture

The purpose of the DAMP is not to catch people out — it is to maintain the safety of aviation operations. A positive safety culture means that everyone in the organisation understands why the DAMP exists and supports it.
