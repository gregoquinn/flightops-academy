# ASIC — Aviation Security Identification Card

The Aviation Security Identification Card (ASIC) is the identity credential required to access the secure areas of a security-controlled airport in Australia. Understanding the ASIC system is essential for your role — you will check, request, and work within the ASIC framework every shift.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** Aviation Transport Security Act 2004. Aviation Transport Security Regulations 2005. The ASIC system is administered by the Department of Home Affairs through the AusCheck scheme.

---

## What is an ASIC?

The ASIC is a photo identity card that confirms the holder has:
- Been background checked by the Australian Government (AusCheck)
- Had their identity verified
- Been assessed as suitable to hold unescorted access to the airside area

It is not an access pass by itself — it identifies who the person is and that they have been cleared. Access to specific areas still requires the appropriate authorisation from the aerodrome operator.

---

## ASIC colours and access

ASICs are colour-coded to indicate the level of access the holder has been assessed for:

| Colour | Access permitted |
|---|---|
| **Red** | Landside and airside (security-controlled areas) |
| **Orange** | Landside only |

Most operational staff at YSNF will hold a red ASIC.

---

## ASIC display requirements

An ASIC must be visibly displayed at all times when the holder is in the security-controlled area. "Visibly displayed" means:

- Worn above the waist
- With the photo facing outward
- Not covered by clothing, equipment, or a lanyard

An ASIC in a pocket does not satisfy the display requirement. If you see a staff member or contractor in the airside area without a visible ASIC, challenge them — or if they are not known to you, treat it as a potential security incident.

---

## Applying for an ASIC

ASICs are issued through the AusCheck background checking scheme. The application process involves:

1. Application submitted through AusCheck at [auscheck.ag.gov.au](https://www.auscheck.ag.gov.au)
2. Identity documents verified by the ASIC issuing body (the aerodrome operator or an approved issuing body)
3. Background check conducted by AusCheck (typically 10–15 business days)
4. ASIC issued if the background check is clear

The aerodrome operator manages ASIC issuance for YSNF. New employees should apply for their ASIC as early as possible — they cannot work unsupervised airside without one.

---

## ASIC expiry

ASICs are issued for a maximum of two years. Before your ASIC expires, renew it through AusCheck. An expired ASIC is not valid — you cannot work airside on an expired card.

---

## Lost, stolen, or damaged ASIC

If your ASIC is lost or stolen, report it immediately to:
- Your employer (airport management)
- AusCheck

A lost or stolen ASIC may have fallen into the hands of someone who could use it for unauthorised access. Prompt reporting allows it to be cancelled in the system.

---

## ASIC checks — your responsibility

As a FOO, you are responsible for checking ASICs of persons entering the airside area under your supervision. When checking an ASIC:

1. Is the card a current, valid ASIC (correct format and colour)?
2. Is the card current (not expired)?
3. Does the photo match the person presenting the card?
4. Is the card displayed visibly?

If any of these checks fail, the person must not enter the airside area unescorted.

---

## Visitor access without an ASIC

Visitors who do not hold an ASIC can access the airside area only under the **continuous escort** of an ASIC holder. Continuous escort means:

- The ASIC holder stays with the visitor at all times
- The visitor is never left alone in the airside area
- The ASIC holder is responsible for the visitor's compliance with airside safety and security rules
- If the ASIC holder needs to leave, they take the visitor with them

There are no exceptions to the continuous escort requirement for non-ASIC holders.
