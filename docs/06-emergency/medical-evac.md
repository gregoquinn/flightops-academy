# Medical evacuation

Medical evacuations (medevacs) are among the most frequent non-routine operations at Norfolk Island Airport. The island's isolation, the age demographic of its population, and the limited medical facilities on-island mean that seriously ill or injured patients regularly require urgent transfer to mainland Australian hospitals. As a FOO, you will be the person who makes the aerodrome side of that transfer happen safely and quickly.

---

## Types of medevac operations

**Scheduled medevac** — arranged in advance with sufficient notice to plan the operation fully. Typically involves serious but stable patients requiring specialist treatment not available on Norfolk Island.

**Urgent medevac** — required within hours. Patient condition does not allow delay. May occur at short notice and outside normal hours.

**Emergency medevac** — immediately life-threatening. Every minute counts. May involve RAAF or other rapid-response aircraft.

---

## Who initiates a medevac

A medevac request typically comes from:
- Norfolk Island Hospital (direct call to FOO or airport manager)
- Medical officer or attending GP
- Airline medevac coordination desk
- Emergency services

You may receive the call at any time, including overnight when the aerodrome is closed.

---

## Your immediate actions on medevac notification

### 1. Gather information

When you receive the medevac request, collect:

| Information | Why you need it |
|---|---|
| Patient condition (stable / urgent / critical) | Determines urgency of your response |
| Number of medical staff accompanying | Affects how many people need apron access |
| Aircraft type and callsign | Affects which bay to prepare, fuel requirements |
| ETA | Tells you how much time you have to prepare |
| Special requirements | Stretcher loading? Oxygen? Isolation procedures? |

### 2. Open the aerodrome if closed

If outside normal hours:
- Activate runway and approach lighting
- Open the operations room
- Confirm all systems are operational
- Conduct a rapid inspection of the runway if time allows

If there is no time for a full inspection before the aircraft arrives, conduct at least a visual check of the runway from the vehicle while activating lighting. Document that a time-critical rapid inspection was conducted.

### 3. Prepare the apron

- Clear the designated medevac bay of all equipment and vehicles
- Position the ambulance where it can reach the aircraft safely but does not block operations
- Identify a clear path from the aircraft door to the ambulance
- Brief ambulance crew on apron safety (see below)

### 4. Advise the inbound aircraft

When the aircraft is in contact:

> *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, runway [number] in use and clear, wind [direction] [speed], QNH [value]. Ambulance standing by apron. Any special requirements?"*

### 5. Coordinate customs and biosecurity

For medevac flights arriving from or departing to Australia, customs and biosecurity arrangements may apply. Check your aerodrome manual and the AEP for the protocol applicable to medevac operations. In most cases, medevac patients have a streamlined biosecurity clearance, but this should be confirmed with the relevant authority when time allows.

---

## Briefing the ambulance crew on apron safety

Ambulance crews are trained medical professionals. They are not trained in aerodrome safety. Before the aircraft arrives, brief them clearly:

- **Where to stand** — give them a specific location to wait that is clear of prop wash and jet blast
- **Where not to go** — specifically, behind the engines and in front of the props
- **When to approach the aircraft** — only after you give the signal (engines shut down, or pilot confirms safe to approach)
- **What to listen for** — your instructions; if you tell them to stop or move, they must do so immediately
- **Equipment control** — stretcher wheels and loose equipment can become FOD; remind them to secure everything

Keep this briefing short, direct, and calm. They are focused on their patient — you are focused on the safety around them.

---

## After the aircraft departs

- Conduct a post-movement apron inspection
- Close the aerodrome again if outside operating hours (follow the normal closing sequence)
- Complete the shift log with full documentation of the medevac operation
- Return all equipment to storage

---

## Overnight and after-hours procedures

The most important preparation for an after-hours medevac is done before you need it:

- Know the aerodrome lighting activation sequence by heart
- Know the gate codes and access routes for emergency services
- Keep your phone charged and accessible
- Know the contact numbers for the airline duty medevac desk and the Norfolk Island Hospital
- Have the AEP at home if you are the on-call FOO

> 💡 **Operational tip:** After your first overnight medevac you will realise how much muscle memory matters. The operations that feel automatic in daylight become unfamiliar at 2am when you are half-asleep. Run through the medevac sequence mentally during quiet periods on shift so that it stays sharp.
