# Emergency plan overview

The Airport Emergency Plan (AEP) is the document that describes how Norfolk Island Airport will respond to emergencies. Every FOO must be familiar with the AEP before their first shift. This chapter summarises the structure and key elements — but the AEP itself, held in the operations room, is the authoritative document.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** CASR Part 139, Subpart F — Emergency planning. MOS Part 139, Chapter 8 — Aerodrome emergency planning. The AEP must be developed, maintained, and exercised by the aerodrome operator.

---

## What the AEP covers

The AEP provides procedures for the following emergency categories:

| Emergency type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Aircraft accident | Crash on or near aerodrome |
| Aircraft incident | Runway excursion, tyre burst, gear collapse |
| Aircraft fire | Engine fire, cabin fire, post-crash fire |
| Disabled aircraft | Aircraft blocking runway or taxiway |
| Bomb threat | Threat to aircraft or aerodrome |
| Fuel spill | Significant fuel release on apron or movement area |
| Medical emergency | Passenger or crew medical event |
| Security incident | Unauthorised access, breach of security |
| Wildlife strike | Bird or animal strike with injury or significant damage |
| Natural disaster | Earthquake, cyclone, severe weather |
| Dangerous goods | Spillage or exposure of dangerous cargo |

---

## Emergency notification chain

When an emergency occurs, the FOO initiates the notification chain. The typical sequence:

```
FOO detects / is notified of emergency
          ↓
Acknowledge and assess
          ↓
Activate emergency services (000 or direct line)
          ↓
Notify airport manager / supervisor
          ↓
Notify airline (if aircraft involved)
          ↓
Notify Airservices / Brisbane Centre
          ↓
Notify ATSB (if accident or serious incident)
          ↓
Notify CASA (if required)
          ↓
Ongoing documentation
```

The first call is always to emergency services if life safety is at risk. Everything else follows.

---

## The FOO's role in an emergency

The FOO is not expected to be a paramedic, a fire fighter, or a structural engineer. Your role is:

1. **Detect and assess** — is this an emergency?
2. **Activate** — call the right services
3. **Coordinate** — be the communication hub between services, aircraft, and management
4. **Control access** — keep unauthorised people out of the emergency area
5. **Document** — record everything, continuously
6. **Support** — give responding agencies what they need to do their job

Do not try to manage the emergency yourself. Your job is to get the right people there and make sure they have what they need.

---

## Emergency exercises

MOS Part 139 requires the AEP to be exercised at regular intervals. This typically involves a full-scale or tabletop exercise involving all emergency services.

As a FOO, you should participate in every exercise. Exercises reveal gaps in the plan before a real emergency does.
