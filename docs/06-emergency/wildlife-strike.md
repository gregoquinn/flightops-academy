# Wildlife strike

A wildlife strike is any collision between an aircraft and a bird, bat, or terrestrial animal. Norfolk Island's diverse bird population, its coastal location, and the proximity of natural habitat to the runway make wildlife strikes one of the most significant and frequently managed hazards at YSNF.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 7 — Wildlife hazard management. CASA Advisory Circular AC 139-06 — Wildlife hazard management at aerodromes. The aerodrome operator must have a Wildlife Hazard Management Plan (WHMP).

---

## Why wildlife strikes matter

A single bird of moderate size ingested into a turboprop or jet engine can cause catastrophic engine damage. A flock of smaller birds can be equally dangerous. Even a single bird striking a windscreen at approach speed can penetrate the glass and incapacitate the crew.

The most dangerous phases are take-off and landing — when aircraft are at low altitude and speed, close to terrain where birds congregate, and when the pilots have the least time to react.

---

## Reporting a wildlife strike

Every wildlife strike — or suspected strike — must be reported. This includes:
- Confirmed strikes where remains are found on the aircraft
- Pilot reports of a strike even if no remains are found
- Near-misses where wildlife was clearly in the flight path

### Immediate reporting steps

1. **Obtain information from the pilot:**
   - Phase of flight (take-off roll, climb, approach, landing roll)
   - Approximate location (runway threshold, mid-runway, above aerodrome)
   - Number and size of birds
   - Any damage to the aircraft
   - Any feathers or remains visible

2. **Inspect the aircraft** (after it is on the ground and engines are shut down):
   - Walk around the aircraft looking for feathers, blood, organic material
   - Check engine intakes, leading edges, windscreen, control surfaces
   - Photograph all evidence found
   - Collect feather or tissue samples for species identification (use a plastic bag)

3. **Inspect the runway:**
   - Check the area of the strike for remains, feathers, blood
   - Photograph and collect samples
   - Record the location

4. **Complete the strike report:**
   - Use the ATSB Wildlife Strike Report form (available at avsr.gov.au)
   - Record all information collected
   - Submit to ATSB within the required timeframe

## Wildlife strike report — key information

| Field | Details to record |
|---|---|
| Date and time | UTC preferred |
| Aircraft details | Type, registration, operator |
| Phase of flight | Take-off, landing, etc. |
| Runway | Which runway, which end |
| Species | Identified or best estimate |
| Number of animals | Single, small flock, large flock |
| Strike location on aircraft | Engine, windscreen, fuselage, etc. |
| Damage | None, minor, substantial, destroyed |
| Effect on flight | None, precautionary landing, emergency |
| Remains collected | Yes / No |
| Photographs taken | Yes / No |

## After a strike — inspection before next movement

If a wildlife strike has occurred on the runway, inspect the runway before the next aircraft uses it. You are looking for:
- Biological remains that could cause a slip hazard or attract more wildlife
- Any secondary FOD created by the strike (fragments from damaged aircraft)

Organic material on the runway may attract scavenger birds — monitor the area and disperse any wildlife that congregates around strike remains.

---

## Serious wildlife strikes

If a strike results in:
- Engine damage or failure
- Aircraft damage requiring maintenance before next flight
- Emergency declaration
- Injury to crew or passengers

Treat as an aircraft accident / serious incident and notify the ATSB immediately.

---

## Post-strike aircraft inspection

The airline or their maintenance representative is responsible for the airworthiness assessment of a struck aircraft. Your role is to:
- Secure the aircraft in a bay
- Provide photographs and witness information to the maintenance team
- Ensure no unauthorised persons access the aircraft
- Document all your observations

Do not advise the pilot that the aircraft is safe to fly. That is a maintenance decision — not yours.

## Wildlife management and prevention

The Wildlife Hazard Management Plan (WHMP) for YSNF documents the strategies in place to manage the wildlife hazard. Your role in implementing it includes:

- **Recording all wildlife activity** — not just strikes. Regular sighting records build a picture of seasonal patterns and help identify when risk is elevated.
- **Dispersal** — using approved dispersal methods when wildlife is on or near the movement area. See Module 7 for species-specific guidance.
- **Habitat management** — reporting conditions that attract wildlife (standing water, long grass, exposed food waste) to management so they can be addressed at the source.
- **Reporting strike trends** — if strikes are increasing or occurring in a particular location repeatedly, it indicates the underlying hazard has not been adequately managed.

> 💡 **Operational tip:** Keep a binoculars in the operations room. Many bird species are difficult to identify at a distance, and species identification on strike reports is important data for national wildlife management research. You do not need to be an ornithologist — "large brown hawk, approximately 50cm" is useful information even without the species name.
