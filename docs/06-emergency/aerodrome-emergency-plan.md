# Aerodrome Emergency Plan

The Aerodrome Emergency Plan (AEP) is the master document describing how Norfolk Island Airport responds to emergencies. As an FOO, you must be familiar with the AEP before your first operational shift — not just aware of its existence, but able to act on it under pressure.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 18 — Aerodrome emergency response. AC 139.C-19 — Aerodrome emergency planning. CASR Part 139, Division 1A.05 — Aerodrome emergency plans.

---

## What the AEP must contain

Under MOS Part 139, Chapter 18 and AC 139.C-19, an AEP for a certified aerodrome must address:

**Emergency types covered**
The AEP must cover each of the following emergency categories:

| Emergency type | Key considerations at YSNF |
|---|---|
| Aircraft accident on the aerodrome | Single runway closure, ATSB notification, limited ARFF resources |
| Aircraft accident off the aerodrome | Cliff terrain, ocean proximity, limited vehicle access |
| Aircraft in-flight emergency | Inbound aircraft, coordination with Brisbane Centre |
| Sabotage / bomb threat | Security protocols, AFPO notification |
| Fuel spill or fire | Containment, no engine starts, ARFF activation |
| Medical emergency | Hospital coordination, medevac activation |
| Disabled aircraft on movement area | Recovery contractor, NOTAM, ATSB notification |
| Structural or building fire | Terminal evacuation, aircraft movement suspension |
| Natural disaster | Sea flooding, high winds, earthquake |
| Dangerous goods incident | Isolation, HAZMAT identification, specialist response |

**Notification chain**
The AEP must specify who is notified in each type of emergency, in what order, and through what means. The notification chain at a minimum includes:
- ARFF / Fire service
- Airport management
- Police
- Hospital
- Airline operations control
- Brisbane Centre / Airservices Australia
- ATSB (where mandatory)
- CASA (where required)

**Command structure**
The AEP defines who is in charge during an emergency and what their authority is. The FOO typically acts as the initial coordinator until a more senior authority assumes command.

**Staging areas**
Designated locations for emergency services, medical, and command operations. The AEP must specify:
- Emergency rendezvous point
- Casualty collection point
- Command post location
- Media holding area

**Communications plan**
Radio channels, phone numbers, and communication protocols for each emergency type.

**Exercise requirements**
The AEP must specify how often exercises are conducted and at what scale. Typical requirements:
- Annual desktop exercise (tabletop)
- Full-scale exercise every two years (or at the frequency specified in MOS Part 139)
- Post-exercise review and AEP update

## The FOO's role in an emergency

Your role is defined in the AEP. Generally it includes:

**Detection and initial assessment**
You are often the first person to detect an emergency — through your own observation, a pilot report, or a call from an emergency service. Your initial assessment determines what response is activated.

**Activation**
You activate the emergency response by notifying the appropriate services in the order specified in the AEP notification chain. This is not optional — you do not wait for confirmation or wait to see how the situation develops.

**Coordination**
You coordinate access to the aerodrome — controlling which vehicles enter, directing emergency services to the correct location, and keeping the movement area clear of unnecessary traffic.

**Communication**
You maintain communication with the aircraft, Brisbane Centre, and emergency services. You provide regular updates to all parties as the situation develops.

**Documentation**
From the moment an emergency is declared, you document every action with the time. This record is critical for the ATSB investigation and any subsequent review.

**What your role is NOT**
- You do not fight fires
- You do not provide medical treatment
- You do not manage the investigation
- You do not speak to the media

## Practical application — what to do in the first 60 seconds

Regardless of the emergency type, the first 60 seconds follow the same structure:

```
0–10 sec   Confirm what is happening — see it, hear it, or receive the report
10–20 sec  Activate the most critical service (ARFF for aircraft emergency)
20–40 sec  Broadcast on CTAF if the movement area is affected
40–60 sec  Begin the notification chain per the AEP
```

Everything after the first 60 seconds is scenario-specific. The AEP tells you what to do next for each emergency type.

## AEP familiarisation — what you must know before your first shift

> ⚠️ **Requirement:** The MOS Part 139 requires that personnel who may be required to act under the AEP are familiar with it. This is not a box-tick — you must be able to act on the AEP without having to read it during the emergency.

Before your first operational shift at YSNF, you must know:

1. Where the AEP is physically located in the operations room
2. The emergency services contact numbers — by memory or immediately accessible
3. The notification chain for an aircraft accident
4. The notification chain for an aircraft in-flight emergency
5. The location of the emergency rendezvous point
6. The ATSB notification number — 1800 011 034
7. When ATSB notification is mandatory
8. Your authority limits — what you can and cannot authorise

---

## AEP review and exercises

The AEP must be reviewed annually as part of the aerodrome manual validation process. Following any emergency or exercise, the AEP must be reviewed and updated if deficiencies are identified.

As an FOO, you may be asked to participate in emergency exercises. These are not optional — they are how you and the response team identify gaps in the plan before they become gaps in a real emergency.

> 💡 **Study tip:** In your competency assessment you may be presented with an emergency scenario and asked to describe the AEP response. Always structure your answer around: activate, communicate, coordinate, document. These four actions apply to every emergency type.
