# Airport layout and characteristics

Norfolk Island Airport (ICAO: YSNF) is a single-runway certified aerodrome located approximately 1km south of the Burnt Pine town centre on Norfolk Island. It is the only aerodrome on the island and the sole gateway for all passenger, cargo, and emergency aviation access.

## Runway

Norfolk Island Airport has a single sealed runway oriented roughly east-west.

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Designation | Runway 11 / Runway 29 |
| Length | 1,951m |
| Width | 45m |
| Surface | Asphalt |
| PCN | Refer to current AIP AD |
| Runway strip width | 150m |

### Runway slope

The runway has a slight overall gradient from the 29 threshold end to the 11 threshold end. The terrain immediately beyond the runway thresholds drops away sharply — this is a characteristic of the island topography and affects aircraft performance calculations, particularly in the event of a runway excursion.

> ⚠️ **Important:** Aircraft departing runway 29 have minimal clearance before terrain. Pilots are aware of this — you should be too when assessing any incidents near that end.

### Threshold displacements

Check the current AIP AD (YSNF) for any current threshold displacements. These change periodically due to resurfacing, maintenance, or obstacle issues.

## Taxiways

Norfolk Island Airport has a limited taxiway system. Aircraft typically taxy back on the runway (backtrack) after landing before vacating to the apron.

| Designation | Location | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Taxy Alpha | Parallel section mid-field | Main access to apron |
| Link taxiways | As charted | Refer AIP AD for current chart |

Backtrack procedures on a single-runway airport require particular attention to inbound traffic. As the FOO, ensure inbound and outbound aircraft are coordinated to prevent a conflict during backtrack.

> 💡 **Operational tip:** When an aircraft is backtacking and you have inbound traffic, communicate the situation clearly to both aircraft. Do not assume pilots know each other's position.

## Apron

The apron at Norfolk Island Airport can accommodate the typical RPT aircraft (ATR 72, Embraer regional jets) plus one or two additional smaller aircraft.

| Bay | Typical use |
|---|---|
| Bay 1 | RPT aircraft |
| Bay 2 | Charter / GA |
| Remote area | Maintenance or overflow as directed |

**Apron management considerations:**
- The apron is exposed to prevailing winds — jet blast and prop wash can affect personnel and equipment
- Fuel is stored on the western side of the apron — observe fire safety distances
- Ground power unit (GPU) access is limited — aircraft may need to run APU
- Baggage handling is manual — no jetway or airbridge

**PAPI — Precision Approach Path Indicator**
Each runway threshold has a PAPI system providing visual glidepath guidance. The standard PAPI indication is:

| Indication | Meaning |
|---|---|
| All white | Too high |
| 2 red / 2 white | On glidepath |
| All red | Too low |

As the FOO, check that PAPIs are functional and showing the correct indication during your opening inspection. A misaligned or unserviceable PAPI is a significant defect requiring a NOTAM.

## Weather characteristics

Norfolk Island's weather is strongly influenced by its isolation in the South Pacific and the surrounding ocean.

### Prevailing conditions
- Trade winds from the east-southeast are the dominant pattern
- The island experiences rapid weather changes — a clear morning can deteriorate to low cloud and fog within minutes
- Sea fog is common, particularly in winter (June–August), and can close the aerodrome with little warning

### Sea fog
Sea fog on Norfolk Island is one of the most significant operational hazards. Characteristics:
- Typically arrives from the east
- Can reduce visibility to below VMC minimums in under 5 minutes
- Often lifts quickly once the sun warms the island surface
- Can be difficult to forecast accurately — always brief inbound aircraft on current visibility trends

### Seasonal patterns
| Season | Typical conditions |
|---|---|
| Summer (Dec–Feb) | Warm, humid, occasional tropical systems |
| Autumn (Mar–May) | Transitional, mixed |
| Winter (Jun–Aug) | Cooler, sea fog risk highest |
| Spring (Sep–Nov) | Improving, winds variable |

### Crosswind considerations
With a single east-west runway, easterly and westerly winds are favourable. A strong southerly or northerly produces significant crosswind conditions. The ATR 72 crosswind limit is lower than that of larger jet aircraft — monitor wind carefully during RPT operations.

## Known operational characteristics

The following are characteristics that experienced operators on Norfolk Island have identified as important to know:

- **Bird activity** is heaviest in the early morning, particularly around dawn. Opening inspections must include a thorough wildlife assessment before the first movement.
- **Whale migration** occurs between June and October. Whale spotting from the aerodrome area is common but has no direct operational impact.
- **Tourism peaks** occur over Christmas/New Year and Easter. Traffic volumes increase significantly and charter operations supplement RPT.
- **Medical evacuations** occur regularly given the island's isolation. The FOO must be prepared to support a medevac at any time, including outside normal operating hours.
- **Fuel availability** is limited to what is on-island. Monitor fuel stock levels and know the contact for emergency fuel resupply.
- **Ground handling resources** are limited. Allow more time for turnarounds than you would at a major airport.
