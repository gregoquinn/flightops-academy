# Wildlife species guide

Norfolk Island's natural environment is rich in birdlife, including several species endemic to the island. Understanding the species present, their behaviour, and their seasonal patterns is essential for effective wildlife hazard management at YSNF.

---

## High-risk species at YSNF

### White Tern (Gygis alba)

**Risk level:** Medium
**Size:** Small (28cm, 100g)
**Behaviour:** Found throughout the island. Commonly seen flying over the aerodrome, particularly at dawn and dusk. Tends to fly at low altitude along the coastline and over open areas.
**Seasonal pattern:** Year-round resident; breeding activity increases in summer.
**Strike risk:** Low individual risk due to small size but flocks can represent an engine ingestion risk.
**Dispersal:** Responds to vehicle movement and noise. Does not persist on the runway surface.

### Black Noddy (Anous minutus)

**Risk level:** Medium
**Size:** Small-medium (35cm, 100g)
**Behaviour:** Pelagic seabird that roosts on the island. Commonly seen in flocks over the coastline adjacent to the aerodrome.
**Seasonal pattern:** Year-round but numbers increase during breeding season (October–March).
**Dispersal:** Generally avoids low-flying aircraft. Responds to distress call playback.

### Masked Booby (Sula dactylatra)

**Risk level:** High
**Size:** Large (85cm, 1.5kg)
**Behaviour:** Large seabird that rests on open ground. Will sit on runway or apron edges if not actively dispersed. Does not react quickly to approaching aircraft.
**Seasonal pattern:** Year-round. Breeding pairs may be protective of territory.
**Dispersal:** Requires active approach — vehicle must drive close. Does not respond well to audio dispersal alone.
**Note:** A Masked Booby strike represents a significant hazard due to the bird's mass. Single strike can cause windscreen penetration or engine damage on smaller aircraft.

### Red-tailed Tropicbird (Phaethon rubricauda)

**Risk level:** Medium-High
**Size:** Medium (45cm body, 80cm including tail streamers, 700g)
**Behaviour:** Distinctive white bird with red tail streamers. Nests in burrows near the aerodrome. Overflies the runway regularly.
**Seasonal pattern:** Year-round resident.
**Dispersal:** Responds to vehicle approach. Does not land on runway surface normally.

### Norfolk Island Green Parrot (Cyanoramphus cookii)

**Risk level:** Low (protected species — special procedures apply)
**Size:** Small (30cm)
**Behaviour:** Endemic to Norfolk Island and critically endangered. Occasionally seen in vegetation adjacent to the movement area.
**Important:** This species is protected under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999. Do NOT use any dispersal methods that could injure or kill a Green Parrot. If a Green Parrot is on the runway, contact the Parks Australia ranger before taking any dispersal action.
**Strike risk:** Very low probability of strike.

### House Sparrow / Common Myna (Passer domesticus / Acridotheres tristis)

**Risk level:** Low (individually) — Medium (in flocks near buildings)
**Size:** Small
**Behaviour:** Common around the terminal and apron buildings. Attracted to food scraps and waste.
**Management:** Manage food waste around the terminal. These species rarely venture onto the runway itself but represent an engine ingestion risk during ground operations near buildings.

## Dispersal methods

### Vehicle movement

The most effective and commonly used dispersal method. Drive the inspection vehicle towards the wildlife at walking pace. Most species will move away. Do not drive at speed — a startled bird may fly into the path of the vehicle or an aircraft.

### Distress call playback

A recorded distress call device plays the alarm calls of the target species, causing other birds of that species to flee. Effective for some species, less so for others. Check your aerodrome's WHMP for approved devices and the species they are effective for.

### Pyrotechnics

Approved pyrotechnic dispersal devices (bird bangers, screamers) produce a loud noise or visual flash that startles birds. Only persons trained and authorised in their use may deploy them. Follow the manufacturer's instructions and WHMP requirements.

> ⚠️ **Important:** Pyrotechnics are fireworks under Australian law. Storage, handling, and use are regulated. Do not improvise or use unapproved devices.

### Habitat modification

Long grass attracts insects and provides cover for small animals. Mowing and vegetation management around the runway edge reduces the food source that attracts many bird species. This is a maintenance function — report overgrown vegetation to maintenance and follow up.

Standing water after rain attracts wading birds and waterfowl. Effective drainage management reduces this hazard. Report any drainage issues to maintenance.

## Wildlife recording

Every wildlife sighting in or near the movement area must be recorded. Consistent recording over time reveals:

- Which species are present
- When they are present (time of day, season)
- Where they concentrate
- Whether dispersal is working
- Whether the overall hazard is increasing or decreasing

Record every sighting in the wildlife log:

| Field | Record |
|---|---|
| Date and time | Always |
| Species | Name or best description |
| Number | Approximate count |
| Location | Specific — not just "runway" but "threshold 11 area, left side" |
| Behaviour | Feeding, resting, flying through, roosting |
| Action taken | Dispersed / not dispersed / monitored |
| Outcome | Birds departed / remained / returned |

## Whale migration

Humpback Whales migrate through the waters around Norfolk Island between June and October. While whales do not directly affect aerodrome operations, their presence:

- Attracts seabirds (particularly boobies and tropicbirds) that follow whale feeding activity
- May bring whale-watching aircraft through the area
- Is a seasonal indicator that bird activity near the coast (and potentially near the aerodrome) may be elevated

During whale migration season, increase the frequency of wildlife monitoring and be alert to elevated bird activity particularly in the early morning.

| Month | Wildlife notes |
|---|---|
| Jan–Feb | Seabird breeding active — booby numbers elevated |
| Mar–Apr | Reducing seabird activity; migratory species passing through |
| May | Quieter period |
| Jun–Jul | Whale migration begins; associated seabird activity increases |
| Aug–Sep | Peak whale migration; maximum associated bird activity |
| Oct | Whales departing; seabird breeding begins again |
