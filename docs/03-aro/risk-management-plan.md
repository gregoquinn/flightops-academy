# Risk Management Plan

The Risk Management Plan (RMP) is the document that describes how the aerodrome operator systematically identifies, assesses, and controls risks to aviation safety. It sits alongside the SMS as a core safety management document and is required under MOS Part 139.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Division 1A.08 — Risk management plans. AC 139.C-27 — Risk management plans for aerodromes. CASR Part 139, Subpart C — Aerodrome operations.

---

## RMP vs SMS — what is the difference?

These two documents are closely related but serve different purposes:

| Document | Purpose | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| SMS | The overall framework — policies, procedures, accountability structures, safety culture | Everything safety-related at the aerodrome |
| RMP | The practical tool — how specific risks are identified, assessed, and controlled | Specific operational risks at this aerodrome |

Think of the SMS as the system and the RMP as the output of that system for identified risks. Every risk that has been formally assessed lives in the RMP.

---

## What the RMP must contain

Under AC 139.C-27, a compliant RMP must document for each identified risk:

**Risk description**
A clear description of the hazard and the potential consequence if it is not managed. Be specific — "wildlife on the runway" is a hazard description; "aircraft engine ingestion of Masked Booby on take-off resulting in engine failure" is a risk description.

**Likelihood rating**
How likely is this risk to occur? Typically rated 1 to 5:

| Rating | Descriptor | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Almost certain | Expected to occur in most circumstances |
| 4 | Likely | Will probably occur in most circumstances |
| 3 | Possible | Might occur at some time |
| 2 | Unlikely | Could occur at some time |
| 1 | Rare | May only occur in exceptional circumstances |

**Consequence rating**
How severe would the outcome be if the risk eventuated? Typically rated 1 to 5:

| Rating | Descriptor | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Catastrophic | Multiple fatalities, total loss of aircraft |
| 4 | Critical | Single fatality, serious injury, significant damage |
| 3 | Significant | Serious injury, major damage |
| 2 | Minor | Minor injury, minor damage |
| 1 | Negligible | No injury, negligible damage |

**Risk rating**
Risk rating = Likelihood × Consequence

| Rating | Level | Action required |
|---|---|---|
| 15–25 | Extreme | Do not proceed. Immediate action required. |
| 8–14 | High | Senior management decision required. Significant controls must be in place before proceeding. |
| 4–7 | Medium | Management responsibility specified. Controls required. |
| 1–3 | Low | Routine procedures. Monitor. |

**Controls**
What is being done to reduce the likelihood or consequence of the risk? Controls must be specific and assigned to a responsible person.

**Residual risk**
After controls are applied, what is the residual risk rating? If the residual risk is still High or Extreme, additional controls are required or the activity should not proceed.

**Review date**
When will this risk assessment be reviewed? Risks should be reviewed whenever the operational environment changes, following an incident, or at the scheduled review interval.

## Worked example — YSNF sea fog

Here is how a formal risk entry in the YSNF RMP might look for the sea fog hazard:

**Hazard:** Sea fog formation at YSNF during early morning RPT operations

**Risk:** Inbound aircraft continues approach in deteriorating visibility below VMC minima, resulting in controlled flight into terrain or runway excursion

**Likelihood:** 4 — Likely (sea fog occurs regularly during winter months, particularly June–August)

**Consequence:** 5 — Catastrophic (CFIT or runway excursion at speed is potentially fatal)

**Inherent risk rating:** 4 × 5 = **20 — Extreme**

**Controls:**
1. FOO conducts visibility observations every 15 minutes during periods of fog risk (dewpoint spread <3°C)
2. FOO broadcasts visibility trend information to inbound aircraft immediately upon detection of deterioration
3. METAR issued at scheduled intervals and on special observation criteria
4. Pilot in command retains authority to decide whether to continue, hold, or divert — FOO provides information only
5. Alternate aerodrome contacts maintained in the operations room

**Residual likelihood:** 2 (controls significantly reduce probability of continuation in inadequate conditions)

**Residual consequence:** 5 (consequence if it occurs remains catastrophic — controls reduce likelihood, not consequence)

**Residual risk rating:** 2 × 5 = **10 — High**

**Responsible person:** Aerodrome Manager

**Review date:** Annually, and following any sea fog related incident or diversion

## Common risks in an YSNF RMP

The following risk categories would typically be addressed in the YSNF RMP:

- Sea fog and low visibility operations
- Wildlife strike — Masked Booby
- Fuel supply chain disruption
- Single runway closure (any closure = aerodrome closure)
- Medevac operations at night or in reduced visibility
- Airside vehicle incursion onto the runway
- Contractor scope creep and works safety
- NOTAM omission or error
- OLS penetration by temporary works equipment
- PFAS contamination management (identified at YSNF fire station as of 2024)
- Terrain proximity on approach and departure

## The FOO's role in risk management

You are a primary source of risk identification data. Every defect you record, every near-miss you report, and every hazard you identify feeds into the RMP.

**Your operational obligations:**
1. Report all hazards to your supervisor — even if you have already managed them
2. Complete shift logs accurately and completely — they are the raw data for risk trend analysis
3. Participate honestly in incident investigations — the goal is system improvement
4. Challenge operations you believe are unsafe — you have the authority to stop an unsafe operation

> ⚠️ **Important:** An FOO who observes a risk and does not report it is contributing to the failure of the safety system. The RMP can only manage risks that are known.

---

## RMP review requirements

Under MOS Part 139, the RMP must be:
- Reviewed as part of the annual aerodrome manual validation
- Updated whenever a new significant risk is identified
- Updated following any incident or serious incident
- Updated when control measures change

The RMP is a living document — not a one-time exercise. An RMP that has not been updated in several years is not compliant and not effective.
