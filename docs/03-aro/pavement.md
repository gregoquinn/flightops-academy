# Pavement assessment

Runway and taxiway pavement is a critical asset. Defects in the pavement surface can cause aircraft damage, affect braking performance, and in severe cases make operations unsafe. As an ARO, you need to identify pavement defects, assess their severity, and know when they require a NOTAM or operational restriction.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 4, Section 4.12 — Pavement surface condition. CASA Advisory Circular AC 139-14 — Pavement surface condition assessment and reporting.

---

## Types of pavement defects

### Cracking

Cracks are the most common pavement defect. They are classified by pattern and severity:

**Hairline cracks**
Fine surface cracks with no displacement or material loss. Low immediate concern but should be monitored as they can progress.

**Block cracking**
A pattern of cracks forming rectangular or polygonal blocks across a large area. Indicates pavement shrinkage or age-related deterioration. Not immediately hazardous but indicates the surface is approaching end of life.

**Alligator (fatigue) cracking**
A network of interconnected cracks resembling alligator skin. Caused by repeated load stress — typically in the wheel track areas. Indicates structural failure of the underlying layers. Significant defect — can progress rapidly to pothole formation.

**Longitudinal cracking**
Cracks running parallel to the runway centreline. Can indicate lane joint failure or differential settlement.

**Transverse cracking**
Cracks running perpendicular to the runway centreline. Can indicate thermal movement or joint failure.

**Edge cracking**
Cracking at the pavement edges. Often caused by water infiltration or lack of edge support.

### Potholes

A pothole is a hole in the pavement surface caused by the loss of material, often beginning with alligator cracking. Any pothole on the runway or taxiway is a significant defect requiring immediate assessment.

**Potholes are classified by depth:**

| Depth | Severity | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Less than 20mm | Monitor | Record and monitor for growth |
| 20mm to 50mm | Significant | Assess impact on operations, arrange repair |
| Greater than 50mm | Hazard | Consider operational restriction or closure |

### Settlement and rutting

**Settlement** is a localised depression caused by compression of underlying soils or pavement layers. Settlement in the wheel track areas is particularly significant as it can cause aircraft to deviate from the centreline.

**Rutting** is longitudinal depressions in the wheel tracks caused by repeated loading. Can affect directional control and, in rain, cause aquaplaning.

### Rubber contamination

Rubber accumulates at the touchdown zone from tyre contact during landings. Over time, rubber deposits can significantly reduce pavement friction, particularly when wet.

The touchdown zone — typically the first 200–500m from each threshold — should be inspected for rubber build-up regularly. When rubber deposits are significant (dark, smooth, non-porous appearance replacing the aggregate texture), a friction assessment or rubber removal should be arranged.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS 139 specifies the friction values below which remedial action is required. See Section 4.13 — Pavement surface friction characteristics.

### Surface ravelling

Ravelling is the progressive loss of aggregate particles from the pavement surface. The surface becomes rough and the exposed aggregate can become FOD. Significant ravelling should be reported to maintenance for sealing or resurfacing.

### Joint and seal failure

Expansion joints in the pavement are sealed to prevent water ingress. Failed seals allow water into the pavement structure, accelerating deterioration. Check joint seals during inspections, particularly after wet weather.

## How to assess pavement defects

When you find a defect, record the following:

| Field | What to record |
|---|---|
| Location | Measured position from a reference point (e.g., "220m from threshold 11, 2m right of centreline") |
| Type | What type of defect (crack, pothole, settlement, etc.) |
| Dimensions | Length, width, depth |
| Severity | Assess using the criteria below |
| Photographs | Take photos where possible — date-stamped images document progression |
| Action | What you did (recorded, marked, reported, NOTAM issued) |

### Severity assessment

**Hazard — immediate action required**
- Any pothole greater than 50mm deep
- Settlement sufficient to cause an aircraft tyre to drop unexpectedly
- Surface failure over a large area in the wheel track
- Pavement break-up producing significant loose material

**Significant — monitor and arrange repair**
- Alligator cracking over a significant area
- Potholes 20–50mm deep
- Rutting visible in wheel tracks
- Significant rubber contamination at touchdown zone

**Minor — record and schedule maintenance**
- Hairline or block cracking
- Minor edge deterioration
- Single failed joint seal

## Reporting pavement defects

### When a NOTAM is required

Issue a NOTAM when a pavement defect:
- Creates a hazard to aircraft operations (any hazard-class defect)
- Reduces the available pavement width
- Requires a speed restriction over a section
- May affect braking performance
- Is being repaired during operations (creates works hazard)

### NOTAM content for pavement defects

A pavement NOTAM should clearly describe:
- Which pavement is affected (runway, taxiway, apron bay number)
- Location of the defect (measured from threshold or other reference)
- Nature of the defect
- Any operational restriction or action required of pilots

**Example:**

*"YSNF Runway 11/29. Pothole 3m right of centreline, 150m from threshold 11. Depth approximately 40mm. Area marked with hazard cones. Caution advised."*

## Pavement friction assessment

Reduced friction (especially when wet) is a significant safety hazard because it extends aircraft stopping distances. Signs that friction may be reduced include:

- Significant rubber contamination at touchdown zone
- Smooth, polished surface texture
- Known aquaplaning reports from pilots
- Pilot reports of extended stopping distances

When friction is suspected to be low, or on a routine schedule, friction testing is conducted using an approved friction measuring device. The results are compared against MOS 139 maintenance planning levels.

**If friction is below the minimum level:** Issue a NOTAM. Advise pilots that braking may be affected. Arrange rubber removal or surface treatment.

---

## Maintenance coordination

Your role as ARO is to identify and document defects, not to repair them. Repair is the responsibility of the maintenance team. However, your documentation needs to be clear enough for maintenance to:

- Locate the defect without needing to call you
- Understand the severity and urgency
- Prioritise the repair correctly

Copy your defect reports to the maintenance team and follow up if significant defects are not addressed in a timely manner. An unrepaired significant defect that causes an incident is a compliance failure — for the operator, and potentially for you personally if you documented it but did not follow it up.
