# FOD management

Foreign Object Debris (FOD) is any object on the movement area that does not belong there and has the potential to cause damage to an aircraft or injury to personnel. FOD management is a core daily responsibility of the ARO and a critical safety function at any aerodrome.

---

## Why FOD matters

The consequences of FOD on a runway or taxiway can be severe:

- **Engine ingestion:** Small objects ingested into a jet engine can cause compressor blade damage, engine failure, or fire
- **Tyre damage:** Sharp objects can puncture or burst tyres on take-off or landing rolls — a tyre burst at high speed can result in loss of directional control
- **Propeller damage:** Propeller strikes on objects cause blade imbalance and structural damage
- **Windscreen impact:** Objects thrown up by tyres or propellers can damage or penetrate windscreens
- **Personnel injury:** Objects thrown by jet blast or prop wash at high speed can cause serious injury to ground personnel

The most dangerous FOD is often the smallest — a screw, a bolt, a piece of wire, or a fragment of concrete can cause catastrophic engine damage.

## Common sources of FOD at YSNF

| Source | Examples |
|---|---|
| Construction and maintenance | Screws, bolts, wire, concrete fragments, tools |
| Aircraft | Panels, fasteners, safety pins, tyre fragments |
| Ground handling | Straps, tags, loading equipment parts |
| Catering and cleaning | Food packaging, cups, plastic bags, cutlery |
| Wildlife | Feathers, bone fragments, nesting material |
| Weather | Branches, leaves, gravel washed onto pavement |
| Visitors and passengers | Personal items, litter |
| Vehicle operations | Tyre fragments, mud, cargo |

## FOD inspection — what to look for

During runway and apron inspections, check systematically. A useful technique is the **zone method** — mentally divide the runway into zones and inspect each zone completely before moving to the next.

Walk or drive slowly enough to spot small objects. On a bright day, angling your view so that the sun is behind you helps objects cast small shadows that make them easier to see. In low light, shine a torch along the surface — small objects will stand out.

**Particularly check:**
- Runway thresholds — the area immediately at and beyond the threshold receives the highest stress and most traffic
- Runway edges — objects tend to accumulate at the pavement edge
- Runway/taxiway intersections — high traffic areas where vehicles and aircraft manoeuvre
- Apron edges and drainage channels — FOD accumulates at drainage points
- Around ground support equipment parking areas
- Around the fuel storage area

## FOD response

### On the runway

If you find FOD on the runway during an inspection:

1. **Stop and assess.** Is the object a hazard to an aircraft that is airborne or about to land? If there is imminent traffic, broadcast immediately.
2. **Remove it if safe.** Pick it up and place it in your FOD bag. If it is too large to remove quickly, place a marker and assess whether the runway needs to be temporarily closed.
3. **Continue the inspection.** One piece of FOD often indicates there is more nearby.
4. **Record.** Note the type of object, location, and action taken.

If you cannot remove the FOD before an inbound aircraft arrives:

> *"All stations Norfolk, runway temporarily unavailable, FOD on runway, standby."*

Clear the object, confirm the runway is safe, then:

> *"All stations Norfolk, runway [designation] clear, available for operations."*

### On the taxiway or apron

FOD on the taxiway or apron is less immediately critical than on the runway but must still be removed promptly. Record, remove, and identify the source if possible.

## FOD prevention

The best FOD management is preventing it from getting on the movement area in the first place.

**Vehicle inspections**
Every vehicle entering the movement area should be inspected before entry. Check that nothing is loose, unsecured, or likely to fall from the vehicle. Check that tyres are not carrying mud, gravel, or other material from outside the airside area.

**FOD walks**
Some aerodromes conduct periodic FOD walks — a line of personnel walking abreast across the runway collecting FOD by hand. While less common at smaller aerodromes, this technique is highly effective after maintenance work.

**Contractor management**
Contractors working airside must account for all tools and materials before leaving the movement area. A missing screw from a maintenance task is a FOD incident.

**Tool control**
In workshop areas, shadow boards (outlines of tools on a board) help identify missing tools. Any tool taken airside must be accounted for before the worker leaves.

**Post-flight checks**
After each aircraft departure, briefly scan the area the aircraft occupied for anything left behind — particularly after maintenance, refuelling, or cargo operations.

---

## FOD recording and reporting

Every FOD find must be recorded. Your record should include:

- Date and time found
- Location (measured position — e.g., "50m from threshold 11, left of centreline")
- Description of the object
- Probable source (if known)
- Action taken (removed, marked, reported to maintenance)
- Who found it

This information is used to identify recurring FOD sources and to improve prevention measures. If you are finding the same type of FOD repeatedly in the same location, that is a management problem, not just an inspection problem — raise it with your supervisor.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** AC 139-01 provides guidance on aerodrome inspection and maintenance, including FOD management. MOS Part 139 Chapter 4 sets out inspection requirements.

## Wildlife as a source of FOD

Wildlife — particularly birds — can create secondary FOD through:

- Nesting material carried onto the runway
- Bone and feather fragments from strike remains
- Prey items dropped by raptors

After any bird strike or wildlife incident on the movement area, conduct a thorough inspection of the affected area for biological material. Feathers and organic matter do not normally cause aircraft damage, but bone fragments and beak material can.
