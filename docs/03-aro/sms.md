# Safety Management Systems

A Safety Management System (SMS) is the formal framework through which an aerodrome operator manages safety. Understanding the SMS is increasingly important for FOOs — it is the system within which all of your inspections, reports, and incident notifications sit. The AVIZ0003 unit of competency includes knowledge of SMS principles as they apply to aerodrome operations.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** ICAO Annex 19 — Safety Management. CASR Part 119 — Air operator certification (SMS requirements). CASA AC 119-01 — Safety Management Systems. While Part 119 applies to airlines, the SMS principles are embedded in the aerodrome operator obligations under Part 139.

---

## What is an SMS?

An SMS is a systematic approach to managing safety that integrates operational procedures, management systems, and safety culture into a unified framework. It is not a set of paperwork — it is a way of operating.

The four components of an SMS, as defined by ICAO:

| Component | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Safety policy** | The operator's commitment to safety — who is accountable and what the safety objectives are |
| **Safety risk management** | The process of identifying hazards, assessing risks, and implementing controls |
| **Safety assurance** | Monitoring whether the controls are working — audits, inspections, performance monitoring |
| **Safety promotion** | Training, communication, and culture — making safety everyone's responsibility |

---

## The safety hierarchy

When a hazard is identified, controls are implemented in order of effectiveness — from most effective (eliminate the hazard) to least effective (rely on the person):

```
1. Elimination      — remove the hazard entirely
2. Substitution     — replace with a less hazardous alternative
3. Engineering      — physical controls (barriers, guards, lighting)
4. Administrative   — procedures, training, signs, checklists
5. PPE/behaviour    — last resort — personal protective equipment and individual vigilance
```

In aerodrome operations, most controls are administrative — procedures, checklists, and training. This means individual vigilance and procedure compliance are critical safety controls, not optional extras.

## Hazard identification — your role

As an FOO, you are the primary hazard identification system at your aerodrome. Every inspection, every shift log entry, every incident report is data for the SMS.

**What constitutes a hazard in the aerodrome context:**
- Physical defects — pavement cracks, lighting failures, FOD
- Procedural gaps — contractors not following the works safety plan
- Environmental conditions — wildlife activity, poor drainage, vegetation encroachment
- Near-misses — events that could have caused an incident but didn't
- System failures — radio malfunctions, gate locks failing, fuel pump issues

**How to report hazards:**
1. Record in the shift log immediately
2. Report to your supervisor
3. Submit to the internal safety reporting system (if your aerodrome has one)
4. For aviation-related hazards, consider filing an ASIR at avsr.gov.au

> 💡 **Operational tip:** Report near-misses as seriously as incidents. A near-miss is a warning that the system is not working as intended — it is far more valuable to investigate it before an incident occurs than after.

## Safety reporting culture

A positive safety culture is one where people report errors, near-misses, and hazards without fear of punishment. CASA and ICAO both strongly encourage voluntary safety reporting.

**Just culture principles:**
- Honest reporting of errors is encouraged and protected
- Reckless or deliberate violations are still subject to consequences
- The goal is to learn from events, not to punish individuals

**Why this matters for you:**
If you make an error — a missed NOTAM, a defect not recorded, a wrong procedure — the worst thing you can do is conceal it. Self-reported errors are treated far more leniently than errors discovered by others. More importantly, concealed errors cannot be corrected or learned from.

## Safety performance monitoring

Part of the SMS is tracking safety performance over time. At the aerodrome level, this includes:

| Metric | What it measures |
|---|---|
| Runway incursions | Number and severity of unauthorised runway entries |
| Wildlife strikes | Frequency, species, location, and damage |
| NOTAM accuracy | Errors or omissions in NOTAM filings |
| Inspection findings | Defect rates, recurrence of same defects |
| Response times | Time from defect identification to action |
| Training currency | Proportion of staff with current ARO/WSO qualifications |

As an FOO, your inspection records and shift logs directly feed into this data. Accurate, consistent documentation is not just a regulatory requirement — it is the raw material for safety improvement.

## Fatigue risk management

Fatigue is a systemic safety risk in aviation operations. The DAMP addresses alcohol and drugs — but fatigue is equally capable of impairing performance to the same level.

Australian aviation operators are required to address fatigue under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 and CASA guidance material.

**Your obligations regarding fatigue:**
- Do not commence a shift if you are too fatigued to perform SSAAs safely
- Report concerns about rostering that creates fatigue risk to your supervisor
- Know your own fatigue indicators — difficulty concentrating, slow reaction times, missing things you would normally catch

**Fatigue indicators to watch for during a shift:**
- Missing items during inspection that you would normally catch
- Making errors in documentation
- Difficulty remembering what you have and have not done
- Slower reaction times on the radio
- Increased irritability or tunnel vision

If you recognise significant fatigue during a shift, escalate to your supervisor immediately. A fatigued FOO on an active aerodrome is a safety risk.

## Change management

One of the less obvious but important aspects of SMS is managing change safely. Changes to aerodrome infrastructure, procedures, staffing, or equipment all introduce risk that must be assessed before the change is implemented.

Examples of changes that require formal safety assessment at YSNF:

- Installation of new lighting or navigation aids
- Resurfacing of runways or taxiways
- Changes to operating hours or movement schedules
- Introduction of new aircraft types
- Changes to emergency service arrangements
- New fuel arrangements or fuel supplier

As an FOO, you may be asked to input into change management assessments. Your operational knowledge of how things actually work on the ground is valuable data that management and safety officers may not have.
