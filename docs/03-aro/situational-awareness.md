# Situational awareness

Situational awareness (SA) is the ability to perceive what is happening around you, understand what it means, and project what is likely to happen next. It is one of the most important non-technical skills an Airport Operations Officer can develop — and one of the most commonly cited factors in aviation incidents when it breaks down.

This chapter addresses the AVIZ0003 unit of competency — Maintain basic situational awareness in an aviation workplace.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** CASA AC 139.C-02 — Aerodrome Personnel. The requirement for situational awareness training is embedded in the ARO competency standards under the AVI Aviation Training Package (AVIZ0003).

---

## The three levels of situational awareness

Situational awareness operates at three levels, first described by aviation researcher Mica Endsley:

**Level 1 — Perception**
What can you currently see, hear, and detect? This is the raw data — the METAR reading, the aircraft on approach, the vehicle on the taxiway, the bird on the threshold.

**Level 2 — Comprehension**
What does it mean? A dewpoint spread of 1.5°C doesn't just mean a number — it means fog risk. An unfamiliar vehicle on the apron doesn't just mean a vehicle — it means a potential security incident.

**Level 3 — Projection**
What is likely to happen next? If the fog is developing and the inbound aircraft is 15 miles out, what will conditions be at the time of landing? If that vehicle continues toward the runway, what will happen?

Most incidents occur when people have correct Level 1 data but fail at Levels 2 or 3 — they see what is happening but don't understand its significance or anticipate where it leads.

## Threats to situational awareness

The following are the most common causes of SA breakdown in aerodrome operations:

### Task saturation
When you have too many things to do simultaneously, you focus on the most immediate task and lose awareness of the broader picture. Classic example: coordinating a fuel spill response while an inbound aircraft is calling and a contractor vehicle is waiting for clearance.

**Mitigation:** Prioritise ruthlessly. Life safety first. Everything else waits.

### Complacency
Familiarity breeds inattention. After conducting the same runway inspection 500 times, the tendency is to stop actively looking and start assuming. Defects get missed. Wildlife goes unnoticed.

**Mitigation:** Use a structured checklist every time. Treat every inspection as if it is the first one.

### Distraction
A phone call during a critical inspection. A conversation that pulls your attention away from the movement area. A notification on your radio that causes you to look away at the wrong moment.

**Mitigation:** Establish a personal discipline — movement area inspections get your full attention. Phone calls wait.

### Fatigue
Reduced sleep directly impairs the ability to perceive, comprehend, and project. Research consistently shows that 17–19 hours without sleep produces impairment equivalent to 0.05% BAC.

**Mitigation:** Know your own fatigue indicators. If you are impaired, you must not perform Safety-Sensitive Aviation Activities.

### Fixation
Focusing intensely on one element of the situation while losing awareness of everything else. An ARO who spots a defect and becomes completely absorbed in inspecting it may fail to notice an aircraft on short final.

**Mitigation:** Scan broadly before focusing narrowly. Confirm traffic status before stopping on the runway.

### Expectation bias
Seeing what you expect to see rather than what is actually there. If you expect a clear runway, you may not register the single piece of FOD that is actually present.

**Mitigation:** Actively challenge your own assumptions. Ask: "What have I NOT checked yet?"

## Threat and error management (TEM)

TEM is the framework used throughout Australian aviation to manage the gap between the ideal (what procedures say) and the real (what actually happens).

**Threats** are events or conditions that increase operational complexity — weather, equipment failures, unexpected aircraft, contractor errors.

**Errors** are deviations from intended actions — missed items on an inspection, incorrect radio calls, wrong vehicle routing.

**Undesired states** are the operational conditions that result from threats and errors that were not caught — a vehicle on the active runway, an uninspected aircraft landing on a damaged surface, a NOTAM not filed before works commence.

The TEM model describes three countermeasures:

| Countermeasure | What it means in practice |
|---|---|
| **Avoid** | Recognise the threat before it becomes an error — plan, brief, check |
| **Trap** | Catch the error before it creates an undesired state — cross-check, verify, read back |
| **Mitigate** | Recover from the undesired state before it becomes an incident — stop, assess, communicate |

> 💡 **Operational tip:** The most experienced FOOs do not avoid errors because they are perfect — they avoid errors because they have built habits that catch errors early. Checklists, readbacks, and verbal confirmations are traps, not bureaucracy.

## Situational awareness in practice — the FOO scan

During operations, develop a personal scan cycle — a routine pattern of attention that ensures you maintain awareness of all key elements throughout your shift.

A suggested scan cycle for an FOO during active operations:

```
Every 2-3 minutes:
  → Check the CTAF — any traffic calling?
  → Visual scan of the movement area — any vehicles, wildlife, or unexpected activity?
  → Check the weather — any changes in visibility, cloud, or wind?

Every 15-20 minutes:
  → Review active NOTAMs — are all conditions still as notamed?
  → Check the schedule — what is the next movement and when?
  → Confirm contractors are in approved positions

After every movement:
  → Did anything happen that requires a log entry?
  → Did the aircraft report anything?
  → Is the runway clear and serviceable for the next movement?
```

## Communication and SA

Poor communication is one of the most direct causes of SA breakdown in the aerodrome environment. Two people can be looking at the same situation and have completely different pictures of it because they have not shared their understanding.

**Closed-loop communication** is the standard in aviation for this reason:

1. Sender transmits the message
2. Receiver reads back what they heard
3. Sender confirms the readback is correct (or corrects it)

This applies on the radio, in person, and in written handovers. If a readback or confirmation is not received, the communication has not been completed.

> ⚠️ **Important:** The shift log handover is a communication event. An incomplete or vague handover creates an SA gap for the incoming operator. Write it as if the incoming person knows nothing about the shift — because effectively, they don't.

## Human factors in aerodrome operations

The following human factors are directly relevant to aerodrome operations and are assessed as part of AVIZ0003:

**Decision making under pressure**
The ability to make sound operational decisions when time is limited and consequences are high. The structured decision-making process is:
1. Recognise the situation — what is actually happening?
2. Identify the options — what can I do?
3. Assess the options — what are the consequences of each?
4. Choose and act — commit to the best option available
5. Review — was the outcome what I expected?

**Assertiveness**
The willingness to speak up when you believe something is wrong, even to someone more senior. In aviation this is referred to as the authority gradient — the tendency for junior crew to defer to senior crew even when they can see an error occurring.

As an FOO, assertiveness means: telling a contractor their vehicle must leave the runway even if they push back; advising a pilot of deteriorating conditions even if they seem impatient; escalating a safety concern to your supervisor even if it feels uncomfortable.

**Workload management**
The ability to prioritise tasks and sequence them appropriately when multiple demands compete for your attention. See the task saturation section above.
