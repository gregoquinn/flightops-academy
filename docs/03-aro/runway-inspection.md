# Runway inspections

The runway inspection is the most critical routine task performed by an Aerodrome Reporting Officer. Its purpose is to confirm that the runway and associated movement areas are in a safe and serviceable condition before aircraft operations begin, and to identify any defects that require action.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 4 — Aerodrome inspections. The minimum inspection frequency, required elements, and documentation requirements are all specified in MOS 139 and the aerodrome's own approved aerodrome manual.

CASR Part 139.090 requires the aerodrome operator to establish a system for inspecting the aerodrome. MOS Part 139 specifies the minimum content of those inspections.

---

## Inspection frequency

| Condition | Minimum frequency |
|---|---|
| Normal RPT operations | Before first movement of the day |
| Increased traffic | As specified in aerodrome manual |
| After significant weather | Before resuming operations |
| After an incident on runway | Immediately |
| Night RPT operations | Before night movements |

The aerodrome manual may specify more frequent inspections than the MOS minimum. Always follow the aerodrome manual.

---

## What the inspection covers

A complete runway inspection covers the following elements:

### Runway surface
- General condition — look for new cracking, settlement, or surface deterioration
- Rubber contamination — accumulated at the touchdown zone
- Water pooling or drainage issues
- Foreign objects (see FOD chapter)
- Any evidence of previous incidents (tyre marks, fluid stains, debris)

### Runway markings
Check that all runway markings are clearly visible and not obscured by rubber, dirt, or fading:

| Marking | Location | Check |
|---|---|---|
| Designation marking | Threshold | Legible, correct number |
| Threshold marking | Threshold | All bars present and white |
| Aiming point marking | 300m from threshold | Visible and white |
| Touchdown zone marking | First 900m | All bars present |
| Centreline marking | Full length | Continuous, white |
| Edge markings (if present) | Both sides | White and continuous |

### Runway lighting
Walk the full length of both sides during low-light or pre-dawn inspections. Check:
- All edge lights are present, unbroken, and aimed correctly
- Threshold lights are green from the approach direction, red looking back down the runway
- Runway End Identifier Lights (REIL) if installed
- Approach lighting if present

### Runway shoulders and strip
- Inspect the runway strip (the graded area each side of the runway) for erosion, soft ground, or obstacles
- Check that no vegetation or objects have grown or encroached above the strip surface level

### Taxiway connections
- Inspect all runway/taxiway intersections for surface condition
- Check that hold short markings are clearly visible
- Check taxiway lighting if applicable

### Threshold area
- Inspect the area beyond each threshold for obstacles, hazards, or wildlife
- Check displaced threshold markings if applicable
- Inspect approach slopes visually for penetrating obstacles

## How to conduct the inspection — methodology

**Vehicle inspection first**
Before the runway inspection, check your inspection vehicle — tyres, fuel, radio, lights, and that your personal protective equipment (high-visibility vest) is on. Your vehicle is also an airside object and must not become a hazard.

**Radio call before entering**
Before entering the movement area, make a broadcast on the CTAF/MBZ:

> *"Norfolk Radio, [callsign], runway inspection, entering runway [number] at [time]. Request confirmation of no traffic."*

Then listen for any traffic. If the aerodrome is designated as a MBZ, a broadcast is mandatory. Check NAIPS/AIP for current designation.

**Drive the full length — both sides**
Drive along one side of the runway at a speed that allows thorough inspection (typically no more than 20 km/h). Return along the other side. Do not cut across the runway centreline unless it is necessary to inspect a specific feature.

**Stop and investigate**
If you observe anything unusual — a discoloured patch, an object, damage to a light — stop and investigate on foot. Photograph defects where possible.

**Depart the runway**
When complete, exit the movement area and make a departure broadcast:

> *"Norfolk Radio, [callsign], runway inspection complete, vacating [exit point]."*

---

## Documenting the inspection

Every inspection must be recorded. The minimum record includes:

- Date and time of inspection
- Inspector's name
- Weather conditions at time of inspection
- Runway inspected
- Condition observed for each element
- Any defects found and action taken
- Time inspection completed

> ⚠️ **Important:** An inspection that is not recorded did not happen for audit purposes. Complete the form immediately after the inspection while the details are fresh.

---

## Defect classification

Not all defects are equal. When you find a defect, you need to assess its safety significance:

| Class | Description | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Hazard** | Poses immediate risk to aircraft | Close affected area immediately. Issue NOTAM. Notify supervisor. |
| **Significant defect** | Affects operations but not immediately hazardous | Issue NOTAM with limitation. Arrange repair. |
| **Minor defect** | No immediate operational impact | Record and schedule for maintenance. Monitor. |

---

## Runway friction assessment

MOS Part 139 requires the aerodrome operator to monitor the surface friction characteristics of the runway pavement. This involves periodic friction testing using an approved continuous friction measuring device.

If friction testing indicates a friction value below the maintenance planning level specified in MOS 139, remedial action is required.

For rubber contamination at the touchdown zone, the aerodrome operator must arrange removal when the accumulation exceeds the limits specified in MOS 139.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 4, Section 4.13 — Pavement surface friction characteristics.

---

## After an aircraft incident on the runway

If an aircraft has had an abnormal event on the runway — heavy landing, tyre burst, runway excursion, tailstrike — the runway must be inspected before the next aircraft uses it. Look specifically for:

- Tyre fragments (blown tyre debris)
- Aircraft components (panels, fairings, antenna covers)
- Fluid trails (hydraulic fluid, fuel)
- Damage to runway surface or lighting

Do not allow the next aircraft to use the runway until the inspection is complete and the runway is confirmed clear.
