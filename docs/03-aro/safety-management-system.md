# Safety Management System

The Safety Management System (SMS) is the formal framework through which the aerodrome operator manages safety as a systematic, organisation-wide discipline. At a certified aerodrome like YSNF, an SMS is required under MOS Part 139 and must meet the standards set out in ICAO Annex 19 and the relevant CASA advisory circulars.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Divisions 1A.06 and 1A.07 — SMS requirements for certified aerodromes. ICAO Annex 19 — Safety Management. Multi-part AC 139.H-06 — Guidelines for preparing a Safety Management System. AC 139.C-26 — Safety management systems for aerodromes.

---

## Why SMS matters to an FOO

An SMS is not a management document that sits in a filing cabinet. It is the system within which you operate every day. Your inspection records, shift logs, incident reports, and hazard notifications are all data inputs to the SMS. Understanding how the SMS works helps you understand why your documentation obligations matter and how your individual actions contribute to organisational safety.

---

## The four components of an SMS

ICAO Annex 19 defines four components of an SMS, each with two elements:

### Component 1 — Safety policy and objectives

**Element 1.1 — Management commitment and responsibility**
Senior management commits in writing to safety as the organisation's first priority. The safety policy names the accountable manager — the person who has ultimate authority and responsibility for safety at the aerodrome.

**Element 1.2 — Safety accountabilities and responsibilities**
Every person in the organisation has defined safety accountabilities. As an FOO, your accountabilities include conducting inspections to the required standard, documenting accurately, and reporting hazards and incidents.

### Component 2 — Safety risk management

**Element 2.1 — Hazard identification**
A systematic process for identifying hazards before they cause incidents. Sources include:
- Routine inspections (your daily inspection records)
- Incident and near-miss reports
- Safety audits
- Industry safety information and CASA bulletins
- Staff observations and suggestions

**Element 2.2 — Safety risk assessment and mitigation**
Once a hazard is identified, its risk is assessed using the likelihood × consequence matrix (documented in the RMP) and controls are implemented to reduce the risk to an acceptable level.

### Component 3 — Safety assurance

**Element 3.1 — Safety performance monitoring and measurement**
The organisation monitors safety performance over time using safety performance indicators (SPIs) and safety performance targets (SPTs). Examples at YSNF:
- Number of wildlife strikes per month
- Number of runway incursions
- Percentage of inspections completed on time
- Number of NOTAM errors or omissions

**Element 3.2 — The management of change**
When something significant changes — new aircraft type, runway resurfacing, staffing change, new fuel supplier — the SMS requires a formal safety assessment before the change is implemented.

### Component 4 — Safety promotion

**Element 4.1 — Training and education**
All staff with safety-critical roles receive appropriate training and maintain currency. This includes your ARO and WSO qualifications, AROC, and any site-specific inductions.

**Element 4.2 — Safety communication**
Safety information is shared across the organisation. This includes safety bulletins, incident learnings, changes to procedures, and feedback on hazard reports. A safety culture where people share information freely is more effective than one where safety is treated as a compliance exercise.

## The SMS document structure

A compliant SMS under MOS Part 139 and AC 139.H-06 typically includes:

| Document | Content |
|---|---|
| Safety policy | Signed commitment from accountable manager, safety objectives |
| SMS manual | Describes how the four components work at this aerodrome |
| Safety accountabilities | Who is responsible for what |
| Risk Management Plan | Documented hazard assessments and controls |
| Hazard register | Running record of all identified hazards |
| Incident register | Record of all incidents and near-misses |
| Safety performance indicators | Metrics and targets |
| Safety committee records | Meeting minutes and action items |
| Training records | Evidence of staff qualifications and currency |

## Voluntary safety reporting

A cornerstone of an effective SMS is voluntary safety reporting — staff reporting hazards and near-misses without fear of punishment. Two systems support this in Australian aviation:

**Internal reporting**
Your aerodrome's internal safety reporting system — typically a form or database where you report hazards, near-misses, and safety observations. This feeds directly into the SMS hazard identification process.

**ASIR — Aviation Safety Incident Report**
Available at avsr.gov.au, the ASIR system allows anyone to report aviation safety concerns directly to CASA. Reports can be made anonymously. CASA uses ASIR data to identify systemic issues across the industry.

> 💡 **Key principle:** Reporting a near-miss or hazard is a contribution to safety, not an admission of fault. An organisation that receives many safety reports and acts on them is safer than one that receives few — because the hazards are being identified and managed rather than ignored.

## SMS and the just culture

A just culture is one where people are encouraged to report safety information including their own errors, with protection from punishment for honest mistakes. It draws a clear line between:

- **Acceptable:** Honest errors, system-induced errors, errors made in good faith — these are investigated and learned from
- **Unacceptable:** Reckless behaviour, deliberate violations, wilful misconduct — these may attract consequences

The just culture principle exists because punishment for honest errors drives reporting underground. When people stop reporting, hazards go undetected and the next incident is more likely to be serious.

## SMS maturity levels

SMS implementation is not binary — organisations move through levels of SMS maturity over time:

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 — Emerging | SMS is documented but reactive. Focus is on compliance. |
| 2 — Managing | SMS is active. Hazards are identified and managed proactively. |
| 3 — Proactive | SMS is anticipatory. Trends are analysed before incidents occur. |
| 4 — Optimising | SMS is embedded in culture. Everyone actively contributes to safety. |

Most regional Australian aerodromes operate at Level 2. An FOO who actively reports, documents accurately, and contributes to safety discussions is helping to move the organisation toward Level 3.

## What CASA expects to see in an SMS audit

When CASA audits an aerodrome's SMS, they look for evidence that:

1. The safety policy exists and is current
2. Hazards are being identified through multiple channels
3. Risks are formally assessed and documented in the RMP
4. Controls are implemented and effective
5. Incidents and near-misses are reported and investigated
6. Safety performance is being measured
7. Changes are assessed before implementation
8. Staff are trained and qualified
9. Safety information is being communicated to staff
10. The SMS is reviewed and updated regularly

As an FOO, your shift logs, inspection records, and incident reports are primary evidence in an SMS audit. Accurate, consistent documentation is not just a personal discipline — it is what demonstrates to CASA that the SMS is functioning.

If you are asked about SMS in your competency assessment, structure your answer around the four components and two elements each. That framework covers every SMS question you are likely to be asked.
