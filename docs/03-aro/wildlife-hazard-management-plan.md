# Wildlife Hazard Management Plan

The Wildlife Hazard Management Plan (WHMP) is a formal document that describes how an aerodrome operator identifies, assesses, and manages the risk wildlife poses to aircraft operations. At Norfolk Island Airport, the WHMP is particularly significant given the island's unique ecology and the presence of species that cannot be managed using standard dispersal methods.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 17 — Wildlife hazard management. AC 139.C-16 v1.0 — Wildlife hazard management at aerodromes (June 2023). CASR Part 139, Division 1A.04 — Wildlife hazard management plans.

---

## What the WHMP must contain

Under MOS Part 139, Chapter 17 and AC 139.C-16, a WHMP for a certified aerodrome must document:

**Species inventory**
A current list of all wildlife species present at or near the aerodrome, including:
- Species name (common and scientific)
- Risk classification (high, medium, low) based on body mass, flocking behaviour, and likelihood of aircraft interaction
- Seasonal presence patterns
- Typical locations on and near the aerodrome

**Hazard assessment**
For each species, an assessment of the risk they pose based on:
- Body mass — heavier animals cause more damage
- Flocking tendency — single strikes vs. multiple simultaneous strikes
- Behavioural response to dispersal — some species are slow to react
- Time of day and season — when is risk highest?

**Monitoring zones**
The aerodrome environment divided into monitoring zones. AC 139.C-16 recommends the aerodrome operator select zones based on the Part 139 MOS requirements but extend or reduce the monitoring radius based on local conditions.

At YSNF, monitoring zones should include:
- The runway strip and undershoot areas beyond both thresholds
- The taxiway and apron areas
- The cliff edges and coastal areas adjacent to the aerodrome boundary
- The vegetation zones adjacent to the runway strip

**Approved dispersal methods**
For each species, the plan must specify which dispersal methods are approved, and critically, which are prohibited. Methods typically include:
- Vehicle approach and movement
- Pyrotechnics (bird scarer cartridges, bangers, screamers)
- Distress and alarm calls (electronic)
- Habitat modification

> ⚠️ **Critical YSNF note:** The Norfolk Island Green Parrot (*Cyanoramphus cookii*) is listed as critically endangered under the EPBC Act 1999. Standard dispersal methods including pyrotechnics and vehicle approach must NOT be used. Contact must be made with Parks Australia before any dispersal action involving this species.

**Recording requirements**
All wildlife observations, dispersal actions, and strikes must be recorded. The WHMP must specify:
- The format of the wildlife log
- The frequency of log review
- How data is used to identify trends and update the plan

**Reporting requirements**
Wildlife strikes must be reported to the ATSB under the Transport Safety Investigation Regulations 2021. The WHMP must document when and how reports are made.

## On-aerodrome habitat management

AC 139.C-16 specifies that aerodrome operators should manage on-aerodrome habitat to reduce wildlife attractants. At YSNF, relevant measures include:

**Grass height management**
Grass height in the runway strip should be managed based on the target high-risk species. For Masked Boobies, which prefer open ground for landing and roosting, maintaining shorter grass may reduce attractiveness of the strip.

**Waste management**
No food waste should be accessible airside. Waste bins must be secured and managed to avoid attracting wildlife.

**Water management**
Standing water attracts wildlife. Drainage should be maintained to prevent pooling in the runway strip and threshold areas.

**Vegetation management**
Species of vegetation that provide food, shelter, or nesting sites for high-risk wildlife should be avoided or removed from the aerodrome boundary.

## WHMP review requirements

Under MOS Part 139, the WHMP must be:
- Reviewed as part of the annual aerodrome manual validation
- Updated whenever a new species is identified at the aerodrome
- Updated following any significant wildlife strike
- Updated when new dispersal methods are approved or existing methods are found to be ineffective

## What an FOO needs to know operationally

You will not write the WHMP — that is the aerodrome operator's responsibility. Your operational obligations under the WHMP are:

1. **Know which species are listed** and their risk classification
2. **Know which dispersal methods are approved** for each species — and which are prohibited
3. **Record every wildlife observation** in the shift log and wildlife log using the format specified in the WHMP
4. **Report strikes immediately** — to the shift log, to management, and to the ATSB
5. **Never improvise dispersal** — only use methods approved in the WHMP for that species
6. **Contact Parks Australia** before taking any action involving the Green Parrot

> 💡 **Study tip:** In your competency assessment, you may be asked to describe the content of a WHMP or to apply it to a specific scenario. Know the six elements above and be able to explain how each one applies at YSNF.
