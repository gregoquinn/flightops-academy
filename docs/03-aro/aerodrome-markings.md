# Aerodrome markings

Aerodrome markings communicate critical information to pilots and vehicle drivers. As an ARO, you are responsible for confirming that all markings are clearly visible, correctly positioned, and in good condition during every inspection. This chapter provides the depth required for the AVIB0002 unit of competency.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 3 — Visual aids for navigation, Section 3.1 — Markings. ICAO Annex 14, Chapter 5 — Visual aids for navigation.

---

## Runway markings

### Runway designation marking

The runway designation marking identifies the runway number at each threshold. It consists of a two-digit number corresponding to the magnetic heading of the runway in tens of degrees.

- Runway 11 = magnetic heading approximately 110°
- Runway 29 = magnetic heading approximately 290°

**What to check:**
- Numbers are legible from the approach direction
- White and clearly contrasting with the runway surface
- Not obscured by rubber deposits, dirt, or fading

### Threshold markings

Threshold markings are a series of white stripes arranged symmetrically about the runway centreline at the landing threshold. They indicate where the landing area begins.

**Number of stripes by runway width:**

| Runway width | Number of stripes |
|---|---|
| 18m | 4 |
| 23m | 6 |
| 30m | 8 |
| 45m (YSNF) | 12 |
| 60m | 16 |

**What to check:**
- All 12 stripes present (at YSNF runway width of 45m)
- Stripes white and clearly visible
- No stripes missing, faded, or obscured

### Centreline marking

A continuous white line along the centreline of the runway, providing directional guidance during take-off and landing.

**What to check:**
- Continuous with no significant gaps
- White and visible
- Not displaced from the centreline by surface settlement or repair work

### Aiming point markings

Two large rectangular white markings located 300m from each threshold (on runways 1,200m or longer). They provide pilots with an aiming point for landing.

**What to check:**
- Both markings present and white
- Located at the correct distance from the threshold
- Not obscured by rubber or fading

### Touchdown zone markings

Symmetrical white rectangular marks either side of the centreline within the first 900m of runway from each threshold. They provide pilots with distance information during the landing roll.

**What to check:**
- All bars present within the first 900m
- White and visible
- Count the bars during inspection — if any are missing, record and report

## Taxiway markings

### Taxiway centreline marking

A continuous yellow line along the centreline of the taxiway, providing guidance for aircraft taxiing. Yellow is used throughout for taxiway markings to distinguish them from white runway markings.

**What to check:**
- Continuous, yellow, visible
- Not obscured by rubber, oil, or water pooling

### Taxiway edge markings

Two continuous yellow lines, each 15cm wide with a 15cm gap between them, marking the edges of the taxiway. Used where the taxiway edge is not defined by a pavement edge.

**What to check:**
- Both lines present and visible
- Not obscured or faded

### Runway hold short markings

The most safety-critical taxiway marking. Consists of two solid yellow lines and two dashed yellow lines across the taxiway, positioned before the runway. The solid lines face the runway — you must not cross the solid lines without authorisation.

**What to check:**
- All four lines present and clearly visible
- Yellow and contrasting with the taxiway surface
- Not obscured by rubber, dirt, or water

> ⚠️ **Important:** A faded or obscured runway hold short marking is a significant safety defect. A vehicle driver who cannot clearly see the hold short marking may cross onto the runway. This must be treated as an urgent defect.

## Apron markings

### Aircraft parking position markings

Yellow markings on the apron indicating where aircraft should park. Include nose wheel stop lines, wing tip clearance lines, and equipment restraint lines.

**What to check:**
- Nose wheel stop positions visible and correctly located
- No markings damaged by vehicle operations

### Apron safety lines

Where marked, yellow lines define areas where ground equipment may be positioned and where personnel may walk safely around parked aircraft.

## Displaced threshold markings

When a threshold is displaced (moved from the physical beginning of the runway), specific additional markings are required:

- **Arrows:** White arrows along the centreline in the area before the displaced threshold, pointing toward the displaced threshold
- **Displaced threshold marking:** White chevron or bar markings at the actual threshold position
- **Closed runway markings:** Yellow X marks (if the area before the displaced threshold is closed to all operations)

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Section 3.1.8 — Displaced threshold markings.

---

## Closed runway and taxiway markings

When a runway or taxiway is closed, yellow X markings are placed at each end and at intervals. An X marking means: no aircraft operations permitted on this surface.

**What to check during works inspections:**
- X markings in place before any closure commences
- X markings clearly visible from the air
- X markings not obscured or displaced

## Marking condition assessment

During every inspection, assess the condition of each marking using this simple classification:

| Condition | Description | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Good | Clearly visible, correct colour, complete | Record as serviceable |
| Fair | Visible but faded or partially obscured | Schedule for repainting, monitor |
| Poor | Difficult to see, significantly faded or incomplete | Report as significant defect, arrange repainting |
| Unserviceable | Not visible or misleading | Report as hazard, consider NOTAM if safety-critical |

**Painting frequency:**
MOS Part 139 does not specify a fixed repainting interval — the requirement is that markings must be maintained to a standard where they are clearly visible. In practice, most certified aerodromes repaint runway markings every 2–5 years depending on traffic volume and weathering.

At YSNF, the combination of UV exposure, salt air, and rubber contamination means markings can deteriorate faster than at inland aerodromes. Monitor condition closely and report to maintenance when markings approach the poor condition threshold.

## Rubber contamination and marking visibility

Rubber accumulates most heavily at the touchdown zone — which is exactly where the threshold, aiming point, and TDZ markings are located. In high-traffic conditions, rubber deposits can completely obscure white markings.

**Rubber over markings:**
- Record the extent and location of rubber contamination
- Assess whether the marking beneath is still visible
- If markings in the touchdown zone are obscured: report to maintenance for rubber removal
- If threshold markings are obscured: this may require a NOTAM depending on severity

---

## Night inspection of markings

Markings are harder to assess at night or in low light. During pre-dawn or post-dusk inspections:

- Use your vehicle headlights and an additional handheld torch to assess marking condition
- Pay particular attention to the hold short markings — these are the most critical at night
- Photograph any markings that appear significantly degraded
- If the overall marking standard makes night operations unsafe, advise your supervisor
