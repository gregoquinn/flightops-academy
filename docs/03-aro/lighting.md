# Lighting systems

Aerodrome lighting is one of the most critical safety systems at any airport. It enables operations in low visibility and at night, and provides pilots with essential positional information during approach and landing. As an ARO, you are responsible for checking lighting systems are operational before every period of use and reporting any defects promptly.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 3 — Visual Aids. ICAO Annex 14, Volume 1, Chapter 5 — Visual Aids for Navigation.

MOS 139 specifies the lighting standards required for each type of aerodrome operation. The aerodrome manual specifies which lighting systems are installed at YSNF and the standards they must meet.

---

## Types of aerodrome lighting

### Runway edge lighting

Runway edge lights mark the lateral extent of the runway and are the most fundamental lighting system for night and low-visibility operations.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| Colour | White (variable intensity), red in last 600m from runway end |
| Spacing | Maximum 60m |
| Location | Along both sides of the runway, within 3m of the edge |
| Intensity settings | Usually three steps: high / medium / low |

**What to check:**
- All lights are illuminated (no dark units)
- No more than two consecutive lights are unserviceable — if three or more consecutive lights are out, a NOTAM is required and the affected runway direction may not be usable for night operations
- Lights are aimed correctly (not tilted or rotated by mowing or vehicle contact)
- Lens covers are clean and uncracked

### Runway threshold lights

Threshold lights mark the beginning of the landing portion of the runway for arriving aircraft, and the end of the runway for departing aircraft.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| Colour | Green (approach side) / Red (runway side — do not land beyond) |
| Location | Across the runway at the threshold |
| Orientation | Green visible to approaching aircraft, red visible looking back down the runway |

**What to check:**
- All threshold lights are operational
- Green is showing from the approach direction (drive up the approach and verify)
- Red is showing from inside the runway (verify from the runway side)

### Runway end lights

Located at the far end of the runway (opposite the threshold), these mark the end of the landing area.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| Colour | Red |
| Visibility | Visible from the landing direction only |

### PAPI — Precision Approach Path Indicator

The PAPI is arguably the most important visual aid for approaching pilots at YSNF. It provides glidepath guidance during approach and must be checked on every opening inspection.

**How PAPI works:**

The PAPI unit contains four lights in a horizontal row beside the runway. Each light is a split-beam unit showing red below the glidepath angle and white above it. The combination of red and white lights indicates the aircraft's position relative to the glidepath:

| Indication | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 4 white | Well above glidepath | Descend |
| 3 white, 1 red | Slightly above glidepath | Slight descent |
| 2 white, 2 red | **On glidepath** | Maintain |
| 1 white, 3 red | Slightly below glidepath | Slight climb |
| 4 red | Well below glidepath | Climb immediately |

**PAPI glidepath angle at YSNF:** 3.0° (confirm in AIP AD YSNF)

**What to check:**
- All four PAPI boxes are illuminated and showing the correct colour split when viewed from the approach direction
- Drive up the approach at approximately 3° to visually confirm the 2 red / 2 white indication
- Report immediately if the PAPI is unserviceable, misaligned, or showing an incorrect indication

> ⚠️ **Important:** A misaligned PAPI can give pilots a false glidepath indication. If you suspect a PAPI is misaligned (e.g., a pilot reports an anomaly, or the indication looks wrong from the approach), take it out of service immediately and issue a NOTAM. Do not leave a suspect PAPI in service.

### Taxiway edge lighting

Taxiway edge lights guide aircraft from the runway to the apron after landing.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| Colour | Blue |
| Location | Both edges of taxiway |
| Spacing | Maximum 60m on straight sections, closer on curves |

**What to check:**
- All lights operational
- No consecutive outages that could cause confusion about the taxiway edge

### Taxiway centreline lighting (if installed)

Green centreline lights on taxiways are used at some aerodromes for low-visibility taxi guidance. Check the aerodrome manual for installation details at YSNF.

### Stop bar lighting (if installed)

Red stop bars are installed at some runway/taxiway intersections to prevent runway incursions. If installed at YSNF, they must be operational for all night RPT operations.

### Approach lighting

Approach lighting extends outward from the runway threshold to guide pilots during the final approach phase. The type installed depends on the instrument approach procedures published for the runway.

**What to check:**
- All approach light bars are operational
- No significant outages that would affect approach guidance
- Confirm type and condition in aerodrome manual

### Aerodrome beacon

The aerodrome beacon is a rotating or flashing beacon that helps pilots identify the aerodrome from a distance and in reduced visibility.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---|---|
| Colour | Alternating white and green |
| Meaning | Civil land aerodrome |
| Operation | Dusk to dawn, or when visibility is reduced |

**What to check:**
- Beacon is rotating and both colours are visible
- Not obscured by foliage or structures

## Intensity control

Most aerodrome lighting systems have variable intensity settings. The correct setting depends on ambient light conditions and pilot request:

| Condition | Typical setting |
|---|---|
| Daylight operations | Off or low |
| Dusk / dawn | Medium |
| Night, clear | Medium |
| Night, reduced visibility or overcast | High |
| Pilot request | As requested |

> 💡 **Operational tip:** High intensity runway lighting can be dazzling to ground personnel. Always warn people on the apron before switching to high intensity.

## Pilot Activated Lighting (PALS)

If YSNF is equipped with PALS, pilots can activate the aerodrome lighting themselves by keying the microphone on the CTAF a specified number of times. This allows operations when no FOO is on duty.

If PALS is installed, confirm it is activated and functional at the end of every shift before you leave.

## Reporting lighting defects

Every unserviceable light must be recorded in the inspection log. The key question is whether the defect requires a NOTAM.

**NOTAM required when:**
- Three or more consecutive runway edge lights are unserviceable on either side
- Any threshold light is unserviceable
- PAPI is unserviceable or suspected of misalignment
- Approach lighting has significant outages
- Aerodrome beacon is unserviceable

**NOTAM not required but still record when:**
- Isolated single edge light is unserviceable (monitor — becomes NOTAM if second adjacent light fails)
- Single taxiway light is unserviceable

When filing a NOTAM for lighting, be specific about location. "Runway edge lighting unserviceable" is not sufficient. Specify: which runway, which side, which position (measured from threshold), number of affected lights.

## Common lighting defects and causes

| Defect | Likely cause | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Single light dark | Lamp failure | Record, schedule replacement |
| Multiple lights dark (same circuit) | Circuit breaker or fuse | Check electrical panel, notify maintenance |
| Light tilted or rotated | Vehicle or mowing contact | Record, arrange realignment |
| Cracked or broken lens | Vehicle contact or vandalism | Remove from service, arrange replacement |
| PAPI giving wrong indication | Misalignment, power issue, or water ingress | Take out of service, NOTAM, arrange inspection |
| Beacon not rotating | Motor fault or power failure | Record, NOTAM if visibility reduced, arrange repair |
