# CASA — Civil Aviation Safety Authority

CASA is Australia's aviation safety regulator. It is an independent statutory authority established under the Civil Aviation Act 1988. Understanding what CASA does — and what it does not do — is essential for anyone working in an aviation role.

---

## CASA's role

CASA's primary function is to maintain and enhance the safety of civil aviation in Australia. It does this through:

- **Rulemaking** — developing and maintaining the Civil Aviation Safety Regulations and associated standards
- **Certification** — certifying aircraft, aerodromes, airlines, maintenance organisations, and individuals
- **Surveillance** — conducting audits and inspections to verify compliance
- **Enforcement** — taking action when safety standards are not met
- **Education** — providing guidance, advisory circulars, and safety promotion

CASA does not operate aircraft, provide air traffic control services, or manage aerodromes directly. Those functions belong to individual operators.

---

## CASA and aerodromes — Part 139

The key regulation for aerodrome operators is **CASR Part 139 — Aerodromes.** This Part establishes:

- The requirement to hold an aerodrome certificate to operate a certified aerodrome
- The obligations of the aerodrome operator regarding safety, inspections, and emergency planning
- The standards for the physical characteristics of runways, taxiways, and aprons
- Requirements for lighting, marking, and obstacle limitation
- Obligations regarding works on or near the movement area

The associated **Manual of Standards Part 139** provides the technical detail. Where Part 139 sets the obligation, the MOS sets the standard to which that obligation must be met.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** CASR Part 139 is available at [legislation.gov.au](https://www.legislation.gov.au). The MOS Part 139 is available at [casa.gov.au](https://www.casa.gov.au).

---

## The aerodrome certificate

A certified aerodrome operates under an **aerodrome certificate** issued by CASA. The certificate specifies:

- The name and location of the aerodrome
- The certificate holder (the operator)
- The category of aerodrome operations permitted
- Any special conditions or limitations

The aerodrome certificate is only valid as long as the operator continues to meet the requirements of Part 139. CASA can suspend or cancel a certificate if it finds evidence of non-compliance.

---

## The aerodrome manual

Every certified aerodrome must have an **aerodrome manual** — a document that describes how the aerodrome is operated and how it complies with Part 139. The manual must be approved by CASA and kept current.

The aerodrome manual is your primary reference for site-specific procedures. It will contain:

- Physical characteristics of the aerodrome
- Operating procedures
- Emergency procedures
- Maintenance schedules
- Contact details

> ⚠️ **Important:** You must be familiar with the aerodrome manual for your specific aerodrome. This handbook provides general knowledge — the manual provides the site-specific detail.

---

## CASA surveillance

CASA conducts periodic audits of certified aerodromes to verify compliance with Part 139. These audits may be announced or unannounced. An auditor may observe:

- Runway inspection procedures
- Documentation and record-keeping
- Staff knowledge of emergency procedures
- Compliance with lighting and marking standards
- Management of airside access and security

The best preparation for a CASA audit is to conduct your operations correctly every day, not to change your behaviour when an auditor arrives.

> 💡 **Operational tip:** If you are unsure whether something you are doing complies with Part 139, ask your supervisor before doing it — not after. CASA takes a dim view of operators who discover non-compliance during audits.

## Reporting obligations to CASA

As a Flight Operations Officer, you may be required to report certain events to CASA directly, or to ensure your organisation reports them. Key reporting obligations include:

- **Runway incursions** — any unauthorised entry onto a runway
- **Wildlife strikes** — any strike or near-miss involving wildlife and an aircraft
- **Aerodrome incidents** — events that affect the safety of aircraft operations
- **Infrastructure failures** — significant lighting outages, pavement failures, or other defects that affect operations

Most of these reports are made through the **Aviation Safety Incident Report (ASIR)** system at [avsr.gov.au](https://www.avsr.gov.au).

> ⚠️ **Important:** When in doubt, report. CASA's voluntary reporting culture means that self-reported incidents are treated far more leniently than incidents discovered through other means.
