# ICAO standards

The International Civil Aviation Organisation (ICAO) is the United Nations specialised agency responsible for setting international standards for civil aviation. Australia is one of 193 ICAO member states and is legally bound by the Chicago Convention to implement ICAO standards in its domestic aviation system.

---

## What ICAO is and is not

ICAO sets the international standards. It does not operate aircraft, manage airspace, or regulate individual operators. Those functions belong to national authorities — in Australia, that is CASA.

When you read a CASA regulation or MOS and wonder why something is done a particular way, the answer is often: because ICAO Annex 14 says so.

---

## The Chicago Convention

The **Convention on International Civil Aviation**, signed in Chicago in 1944, is the treaty that created the international framework for civil aviation. Its key principles relevant to aerodrome operations are:

- Each state has sovereignty over its own airspace
- Each state is responsible for the safety oversight of civil aviation within its territory
- States agree to adopt common standards to facilitate safe international operations

Australia's domestic aviation legislation — the Civil Aviation Act 1988 — gives effect to Australia's Chicago Convention obligations.

---

## ICAO Annex 14 — Aerodromes

**Annex 14** is the most directly relevant ICAO document for aerodrome operations. It sets international standards for:

- Aerodrome physical characteristics (runway dimensions, clearances, slopes)
- Visual aids — lighting, markings, and signs
- Rescue and fire fighting
- Obstacle limitation surfaces
- Aerodrome maintenance

Annex 14 comes in two volumes:

| Volume | Coverage |
|---|---|
| Volume 1 | Aerodrome design and operations |
| Volume 2 | Heliports |

Most of what you will read in MOS Part 139 directly mirrors Annex 14 Volume 1. Where Australian regulations differ from Annex 14, those differences are published in AIP GEN 1.7.

---

## ICAO Doc 9981 — PANS-Aerodromes

**PANS-Aerodromes** (Procedures for Air Navigation Services — Aerodromes) complements Annex 14 by providing detailed procedures for aerodrome operations. Key content includes:

- Aerodrome inspection procedures
- Surface condition assessment and reporting (SNOWTAM and RCAM)
- Runway surface friction assessment
- Wildlife management
- Aerodrome works safety

Where Annex 14 is about physical standards, Doc 9981 is about operational procedures. Both are relevant to your daily work as a FOO.

---

## Obstacle Limitation Surfaces (OLS)

One of the most practically important ICAO concepts for an FOO is the **Obstacle Limitation Surface (OLS)** — the imaginary three-dimensional airspace around an aerodrome within which obstacles must not penetrate.

ICAO defines several OLS types in Annex 14:

| Surface | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Conical surface** | Surrounds the outer edge of the horizontal surface |
| **Horizontal surface** | Flat surface above the aerodrome and its surrounds |
| **Approach surface** | Slopes upward from the runway threshold |
| **Transitional surface** | Connects the approach and horizontal surfaces |
| **Take-off climb surface** | Slopes upward from the end of the take-off run |
| **Inner approach surface** | Applies when precision approach lighting is installed |
| **Inner transitional surface** | Applies at precision approach aerodromes |
| **Balked landing surface** | Protects missed approach climb |

As a WSO, you will need to assess whether proposed works — cranes, vehicles, temporary structures — penetrate any OLS. If they do, the work requires a NOTAM and may require a temporary runway closure.

> ⚠️ **Important:** An obstacle penetrating the OLS does not automatically close the aerodrome. It does require a NOTAM, and it may trigger a procedure change or a minimum altitude increase. Always assess OLS penetrations against the current aerodrome manual.

## ICAO aerodrome reference codes

ICAO uses an **aerodrome reference code** to classify aerodromes by the aircraft they are designed to serve. The code consists of two elements:

**Code number (1–4)** — based on the reference field length of the critical aircraft:

| Code | Reference field length |
|---|---|
| 1 | Less than 800m |
| 2 | 800m to less than 1200m |
| 3 | 1200m to less than 1800m |
| 4 | 1800m and above |

**Code letter (A–F)** — based on the wingspan and outer main gear width of the critical aircraft:

| Letter | Wingspan |
|---|---|
| A | Up to 15m |
| B | 15m to less than 24m |
| C | 24m to less than 36m |
| D | 36m to less than 52m |
| E | 52m to less than 65m |
| F | 65m to less than 80m |

Norfolk Island Airport has a code **4C** aerodrome — designed to serve aircraft with a reference field length of 1800m or more, with wingspans up to 36m. This includes aircraft like the ATR 72 and similar turboprops operating RPT services.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** The aerodrome reference code for Norfolk Island Airport is confirmed in the AIP AD section for YSNF.
