# Standard phraseology

Clear, standardised radio communication prevents accidents. Non-standard phraseology — using ambiguous, colloquial, or incorrect language on the radio — has been a contributing factor in numerous aviation accidents worldwide. As a FOO, your radio technique sets the standard for your aerodrome.

---

## The principles of good radio technique

**Clarity** — speak clearly and at a measured pace. Do not rush.

**Conciseness** — say what needs to be said and stop. Unnecessary words block the frequency for others.

**Accuracy** — only transmit information you know to be correct. If you are uncertain, say so.

**Standard phraseology** — use ICAO standard words and phrases. They exist because they are unambiguous in any English accent.

---

## The transmission sequence

Every radio transmission should follow this structure:

```
[Who you are calling] + [Who you are] + [Message]
```

**Example:**
> *"Norfolk Traffic, Golf Foxtrot Alpha Bravo Charlie, 10 miles on final runway 11."*

For a ground station calling an aircraft:
> *"Golf Foxtrot Alpha Bravo Charlie, Norfolk Radio, wind 090 at 12, runway 11 clear."*

## Standard words and phrases

| Word/Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Affirm** | Yes |
| **Negative** | No |
| **Roger** | I have received your last transmission |
| **Wilco** | I understand and will comply |
| **Say again** | Please repeat your last transmission |
| **Standby** | Wait — I will call you |
| **Correction** | I made an error — the correct version is... |
| **Check** | Examine a system or procedure (no reply expected) |
| **Confirm** | Have I correctly received the following? |
| **Acknowledge** | Let me know you have received this message |
| **Go ahead** | I am ready to receive your message |
| **Out** | This communication is complete — no reply expected |
| **Over** | I have finished transmitting — a reply is expected |
| **Mayday** | International distress signal — life-threatening emergency |
| **Pan-Pan** | International urgency signal — urgent, not immediately life-threatening |

> ⚠️ **Important:** Never use "okay", "yeah", "no worries", or other colloquial words in place of standard phraseology. They can be misunderstood — particularly by pilots whose first language is not English.

## Numbers and letters

### Numbers
Say each digit individually for most numbers:
- Runway 11 → "One One"
- QNH 1013 → "One Zero One Three"
- Wind 090 at 12 knots → "Zero Niner Zero at One Two"
- Altitude 5,000ft → "Five Thousand"

Exception: hundreds and thousands may be spoken as "hundred" and "thousand" when unambiguous.

## Readback requirements

Certain critical information must be read back by the receiving station to confirm accurate receipt. At a non-controlled aerodrome, readback is good practice even when not strictly required.

Always read back:
- Runway in use
- QNH
- Any traffic information
- Emergency instructions

**Example — information provision and readback:**

FOO: *"Golf Alpha Bravo Charlie, Norfolk Radio, runway 11 in use, wind 110 at 08 knots, QNH 1016."*

Pilot: *"Runway 11, QNH 1016, Golf Alpha Bravo Charlie."*

FOO: *"Golf Alpha Bravo Charlie, readback correct."*

## Norfolk Island CTAF broadcasts

At Norfolk Island, the FOO uses the CTAF to provide aerodrome information to inbound and outbound traffic. Unlike ATC clearances, CTAF broadcasts are information only — you are not giving authority, only information.

### Standard arrival information broadcast

> *"All stations Norfolk, [vehicle/station callsign], runway [number] in use, wind [direction] [speed] knots, QNH [value], [any significant information]. Norfolk Radio."*

### Standard traffic information broadcast

When a pilot calls inbound:

> *"[Aircraft callsign], Norfolk Radio, runway [number], wind [direction] [speed] knots, QNH [value], [traffic information if applicable], Norfolk Radio."*

### FOD or hazard broadcast

> *"All stations Norfolk, runway inspection in progress, runway temporarily unavailable. Standby."*

When clear:

> *"All stations Norfolk, runway [designation] clear, available for operations. Norfolk Radio."*

## Emergency broadcasts

### Receiving a Mayday

If an aircraft transmits a Mayday on your frequency:

1. Do not transmit immediately — let the pilot complete their transmission
2. Acknowledge: *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, Mayday acknowledged, runway [number] clear, advise intentions."*
3. Activate emergency services
4. Maintain radio contact with the aircraft

### Transmitting a Pan-Pan on behalf of a pilot

If a pilot advises you of an urgent situation that is not yet life-threatening:

> *"Pan-Pan, Pan-Pan, Pan-Pan. All stations Norfolk. [Aircraft callsign], [nature of urgency], [position], [intentions]."*

### Guard frequency — 121.5 MHz

121.5 MHz is the international emergency frequency. Monitor it continuously if your equipment allows. Any aircraft transmitting Mayday on 121.5 should be acknowledged and Brisbane Centre notified.

## Common mistakes to avoid

| Mistake | Correct approach |
|---|---|
| Using "over and out" | Use "over" (reply expected) or "out" (no reply expected) — never both |
| Saying "affirmative" instead of "affirm" | "Affirm" is the correct word |
| Transmitting on a busy frequency without pausing to listen first | Always pause and listen before transmitting |
| Transmitting unclear or ambiguous runway information | Be specific: "Runway 11 in use", not "runway one one" if there's any chance of misunderstanding |
| Not reading back critical information | Always read back runway, QNH, and emergency instructions |
| Transmitting too fast | Slow down — pilots are writing this down or entering it |
