# Emergency communications

Emergency radio communication is the situation where clear, calm, correct phraseology matters most — and where the consequences of confusion are highest. This chapter covers the communications aspects of emergency management. The full emergency response procedures are in Module 6.

---

## The golden rule

In an emergency, your voice must be calm even when you are not. Pilots under stress take their cue from ground stations. A calm, authoritative voice from the FOO helps a pilot stay focused. A panicked or confused voice adds to their workload.

Practise saying these calls out loud. When the real emergency comes, you want them to be automatic.

---

## Receiving a Mayday

**Step 1 — Listen**
Let the pilot complete their initial Mayday call. Do not interrupt. You need all the information they are providing.

A Mayday call typically follows this format:
> *"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday. [Callsign]. [Nature of emergency]. [Position or intentions]. [Persons on board]. [Fuel]."*

Not all pilots will provide all this information in the first call, particularly if they are highly task-saturated. Do not worry — you can ask for it after acknowledging.

**Step 2 — Acknowledge**

> *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, Mayday acknowledged. Runway [number] clear. Pass your details."*

**Step 3 — Obtain information (if not already provided)**

> *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, confirm souls on board and fuel remaining."*

**Step 4 — Activate emergency services**

Do this as soon as you have acknowledged the aircraft. You can continue the radio exchange while making the call — or if you are alone, give the pilot a brief standby:

> *"[Callsign], standby, activating emergency services."*

Then make the call to the fire service and any other emergency services.

**Step 5 — Maintain contact**

Keep radio contact with the aircraft throughout. Provide:
- Confirmation that the runway is clear
- Current wind and QNH if the pilot needs it
- Any information about emergency services positioning
- Reassurance that you are there

## Receiving a Pan-Pan

A Pan-Pan indicates urgency — the situation is serious but not (at this point) life-threatening. Common triggers:

- Engine rough-running or partial failure
- Navigation equipment failure
- Medical situation on board
- Fuel concern
- Unknown aircraft problem

**Acknowledge:**

> *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, Pan-Pan acknowledged. Runway [number] available. Pass your details."*

A Pan-Pan may escalate to a Mayday — stay alert and be ready to upgrade your response.

---

## Declaring an emergency on behalf of an aircraft

If you observe or receive reports of an aircraft in difficulty but the aircraft has not declared an emergency themselves (perhaps their radio is not working), you can declare an emergency on their behalf:

> *"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday. Norfolk Radio. [Callsign or aircraft type if unknown], [nature of emergency as you understand it], [last known position]. All stations, clear the frequency."*

Notify Brisbane Centre immediately.

---

## Notifying Brisbane Centre

In a declared emergency at YSNF, notify Brisbane Centre as soon as possible. Use HF or satellite phone if VHF contact is not possible.

Provide:
- Your aerodrome (YSNF)
- The aircraft callsign and type
- Nature of the emergency
- Last known position of the aircraft
- Your contact frequency and phone number

Brisbane Centre will coordinate with other aircraft in the area and will be your connection to higher-level emergency management.

---

## Radio silence

If necessary, you can call radio silence on the frequency to clear it for emergency communications:

> *"All stations, all stations, all stations. Norfolk Radio. Silence, Silence, Silence on [frequency]. Emergency in progress."*

When the emergency is resolved:

> *"All stations. Norfolk Radio. Distress traffic ended. Resume normal communications on [frequency]."*

---

## After the emergency

When the immediate emergency is resolved, complete the following:

- Record all times and communications in the shift log
- Notify ATSB if an accident or serious incident occurred (1800 011 034)
- Preserve radio recordings if your system has them
- Complete an Aviation Safety Incident Report (ASIR) at avsr.gov.au
- Brief your supervisor
- Debrief yourself — what went well, what would you do differently?
