# Scenario exercises

These scenarios are based on realistic situations encountered in airport operations roles in Australia. Read each scenario, think through your response, then click to reveal the answer.

## Module 1 — Aviation fundamentals

<details>
<summary>Scenario 1 — A pilot asks about a taxiway closure you're not aware of</summary>

A pilot calls you on the CTAF and asks whether a particular section of taxiway is closed. You are not aware of a closure. Where do you check first, and what do you say to the pilot while you are checking?

**Answer:**

Check NAIPS for active NOTAMs applicable to YSNF. While you are checking, be honest with the pilot:

> *"Stand by, confirming NOTAM status."*

Do not guess or give an answer you are not certain of. If no NOTAM is active, the taxiway is not notamed closed — but you should also visually confirm it is serviceable before saying so.

**Key principle:** Never give a pilot information you cannot verify. "Stand by" is a complete and professional response while you check.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 2 — A CASA officer arrives unannounced</summary>

A CASA officer arrives unannounced and asks to see your aerodrome manual. What do you do?

**Answer:**

Provide it. CASA has the right under the Civil Aviation Act 1988 to inspect documents and facilities at certified aerodromes. The aerodrome manual must be current and accessible at the aerodrome at all times.

If you are uncertain the version on site is current, say so and offer to verify with your supervisor.

**Key principle:** Cooperate fully and immediately. Hesitation or obstruction makes a routine visit into a compliance problem.

</details>

## Module 2 — Daily operations

<details>
<summary>Scenario 3 — Four consecutive runway edge lights unserviceable before first movement</summary>

You are 20 minutes into your opening inspection when you find four consecutive runway edge lights unserviceable on the left side of runway 11. The first scheduled aircraft is inbound in 40 minutes. What do you do?

**Answer:**

1. **Record the defect** — exact location, time, and nature (e.g. "Left edge lights unserviceable, positions 3–6 from threshold 11, 4 consecutive")
2. **Assess severity** — MOS Part 139 specifies no more than two consecutive edge lights should be unserviceable. Four is above the permitted threshold and requires action
3. **Issue a NOTAM** — advise of the unserviceable lights and any operational limitation on runway 11 night operations
4. **Contact maintenance** — arrange repair as a priority
5. **Advise the inbound aircraft** — broadcast the NOTAM or advise directly on radio
6. **Document everything** in the shift log with times

**Key principle:** The NOTAM must be issued before the aircraft arrives, not after. If you are unsure whether night ops will be required, issue it anyway — you can cancel it once the lights are repaired.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 4 — Unrecognised vehicle on the apron without a visible permit</summary>

During operations, you observe a vehicle on the apron that you do not recognise and that does not appear to have an airside permit displayed. What do you do?

**Answer:**

1. Approach the vehicle or contact its occupants immediately
2. Ask to see their **airside vehicle permit** and **ASIC**
3. If they cannot produce either, escort them to the terminal and record the incident
4. If they refuse to comply or behave suspiciously, treat it as a security incident and follow the aerodrome security plan
5. Document everything in the shift log

**Key principle:** Every vehicle on the movement area must be authorised. "I didn't notice" is not a defence in a CASA audit or a security investigation.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 5 — Unserviceable lighting in previous shift log but no NOTAM issued</summary>

You arrive for the morning shift and find the shift log from the previous evening shows runway edge lighting was unserviceable but no NOTAM was issued. The first aircraft is 25 minutes away.

**Answer:**

1. **Issue the NOTAM immediately** — the defect exists regardless of whether the previous shift filed it. Your obligation is current, not retrospective
2. **Inspect the lights yourself** during your opening check to confirm the defect
3. **Contact maintenance** to arrange repair
4. **Advise the inbound aircraft** via broadcast or direct radio
5. **Record in your shift log** that you found the omission and the action you took
6. **Report the omission** to your supervisor — a missed NOTAM is a compliance failure that needs to be addressed

**Key principle:** You are responsible for the shift you are on. Fix it now, report it properly, and let management handle the previous shift.

</details>

## Module 3 — Aerodrome Reporting Officer

<details>
<summary>Scenario 6 — Possible tyre problem on landing, next aircraft 15 minutes away</summary>

An inbound ATR-72 reports a possible tyre problem on landing. The aircraft vacates the runway normally and taxies to the apron. The next inbound aircraft is 15 minutes out. What do you do?

**Answer:**

1. **Broadcast immediately:**
   > *"All stations Norfolk, runway inspection in progress, runway temporarily unavailable, standby."*
2. **Conduct an immediate runway inspection** — look for tyre fragments, rubber debris, surface damage, fluid trails
3. **If the runway is clear** — reopen and advise the inbound:
   > *"All stations Norfolk, runway 11 clear, available for operations."*
4. **If debris is found** — clear it, complete the inspection, then reopen
5. **Notify the airline** that the aircraft may require a tyre inspection before its next flight
6. **Document everything** — pilot report, inspection time, findings, action taken

**Key principle:** Never let the next aircraft use the runway until you have physically inspected it. 15 minutes is enough time if you start immediately.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 7 — Three Cape Pigeons on the runway, no movement for 45 minutes</summary>

During your runway inspection you find three Cape Pigeons feeding near the threshold 11 area. There are no movements for 45 minutes. What do you do?

**Answer:**

1. **Record the sighting** in the wildlife log — species, number, location, time
2. **Attempt dispersal** using your aerodrome's approved methods
3. **Monitor** — confirm birds have left the movement area
4. **Do not relax your vigilance** — birds return. Check again closer to the movement time
5. **If birds cannot be dispersed** as the movement approaches, assess the risk and consider a short delay while you make another dispersal attempt
6. **If this is a recurring pattern**, raise it with your supervisor as a habitat management issue

**Key principle:** Dispersal is not a one-time action. Monitor and re-check. A bird dispersed at 0545 may be back by 0600.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 8 — Unidentified metal fragment on the runway, 20 minutes to next movement</summary>

During your afternoon inspection you find a piece of metal approximately 5cm long on the runway centreline, 200m from threshold 29. You don't recognise what it is from. The next aircraft movement is in 20 minutes.

**Answer:**

1. **Pick it up** and place it in your FOD bag
2. **Photograph it** before removal if possible
3. **Record the find** — time, location, description
4. **Continue the inspection** — one piece of FOD often means there is more nearby
5. **Investigate the source** — report to maintenance so they can determine whether it came from recent work or an aircraft
6. **No NOTAM required** for FOD that has been removed and the runway is confirmed clear

**Key principle:** Remove it, record it, investigate the source. An unidentified metal fragment could indicate a maintenance issue or aircraft component loss — both need follow-up.

</details>

## Module 4 — Works Safety Officer

<details>
<summary>Scenario 9 — Contractor wants to bring a 12-metre elevated work platform airside</summary>

A contractor asks to bring a 12-metre elevated work platform onto the apron to replace a floodlight. What do you do before authorising them to bring it airside?

**Answer:**

1. **Get the extended height** in writing — the machine stowed height is irrelevant; the extended boom height is what matters for OLS assessment
2. **Plot the OLS** — determine whether the extended equipment at the proposed position would penetrate any Obstacle Limitation Surface
3. **If OLS penetration occurs** — assess whether operations can continue with a NOTAM, or whether the work must be scheduled during a runway closure
4. **Issue a NOTAM** describing the obstruction — position, maximum height, period of works
5. **Brief the contractor** on airside rules: ASIC, vehicle permit, radio, speed limit, no independent movement
6. **Monitor throughout** — be present or have a representative present during all works

**Key principle:** OLS assessment before anything else. The height when stowed is irrelevant — it's the extended height at the working position that matters.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 10 — Contractor working outside their approved area</summary>

You are supervising a maintenance crew replacing a taxiway edge light. You notice two workers have walked 80m further along the taxiway and are examining a section of pavement not part of the approved works.

**Answer:**

1. **Stop them immediately** — call them back by radio or in person
2. **Confirm the approved scope** — their authorisation covers the light replacement only
3. **Explain the reason** — the works safety plan and NOTAM cover a specific area; working outside it is both unauthorised and potentially unsafe
4. **If they've spotted a real defect** — they report it to you and you will inspect and schedule it through the proper process
5. **Document the incident** in the shift log
6. **Advise your supervisor** — scope creep from contractors is a safety management issue

**Key principle:** Your authority over the movement area is absolute. Scope creep is one of the most common ways airside safety controls are undermined.

</details>

## Module 5 — Radio communications

<details>
<summary>Scenario 11 — Pilot asks you to say again the runway in use</summary>

You are broadcasting on the CTAF and an aircraft responds with: *"Norfolk Traffic, [callsign], say again runway in use."* What has likely happened and how do you respond?

**Answer:**

Most likely your original broadcast was unclear, stepped on by another transmission, or the aircraft was outside range and only caught part of it.

**Response:**
> *"[Callsign], Norfolk Traffic, runway in use runway one one, wind zero niner zero at one two knots, QNH one zero one four."*

**Key points:**
- Speak at a measured pace — slower than you think you need to
- Say "runway one one" — digits individually, not "runway eleven"
- Provide the full information block, not just the runway number
- Do not apologise or explain — just give the information clearly

**Key principle:** "Say again" is a normal request, not a criticism. Respond calmly and completely.

</details>

## Module 6 — Emergency management

<details>
<summary>Scenario 12 — Mayday declared, engine failure on approach, you are alone on shift</summary>

An aircraft on approach transmits: *"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday, [callsign], engine failure, continuing approach."* You are the only person on shift. What are your immediate actions in priority order?

**Answer:**

**Do these in this exact sequence:**

1. **Acknowledge:**
   > *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, Mayday acknowledged, runway 11 clear, emergency services being activated."*

2. **Activate emergency services** — call Norfolk Island Fire Service. Give them: aircraft type, engine failure, ETA, persons on board (ask the aircraft if you don't have it yet)

3. **Clear the runway** — confirm no other aircraft or vehicles are present

4. **Maintain radio contact:**
   > *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, confirm souls on board and fuel remaining."*

5. **Notify the airline** and other relevant parties

6. **Position yourself** where you can observe the landing and direct emergency services

7. **Document** — start writing times and actions as soon as the immediate situation allows

**Key principle:** Acknowledge first, activate services second, everything else after. You cannot do everything at once — the priority order saves lives.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 13 — Nose gear collapsed on runway, fire services on scene</summary>

After a declared emergency landing, the aircraft is on the runway with the nose gear collapsed. Fire services are on scene. No other movements for two hours. What is your next priority?

**Answer:**

1. **Confirm fire risk is managed** — your role is now coordination, not suppression
2. **Notify supervisor and aerodrome operator**
3. **Issue a NOTAM** — runway closed, disabled aircraft on runway
4. **Notify Brisbane Centre** that the aerodrome is closed
5. **Notify the ATSB** — 1800 011 034. Mandatory notification. Do this early, not last
6. **Coordinate aircraft recovery** — the aircraft owner/operator or their insurer's recovery contractor is responsible for removal
7. **Preserve the scene** — no unauthorised vehicles on or near the aircraft until ATSB authorises movement
8. **Document continuously** — times, communications, persons on scene, all actions

**Key principle:** The ATSB notification is mandatory and time-sensitive. Do not wait until the aircraft is cleared before calling them.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 14 — Medevac request received after the aerodrome is closed for the night</summary>

You have completed the closing inspection and are writing up the shift log when the Norfolk Island Hospital calls. They have a critically ill patient requiring urgent medevac. A medevac aircraft is two hours away.

**Answer:**

1. **Confirm details** — patient condition, number of medical staff, aircraft type and callsign, ETA
2. **Contact the airline medevac coordination desk** to arrange the aircraft
3. **Reopen the aerodrome:**
   - Activate all runway and approach lighting
   - Open the operations room
   - Conduct a runway inspection — you have two hours, use it properly
4. **Coordinate ambulance access** — confirm gate access and waiting position on the apron
5. **Notify Brisbane Centre** that YSNF is open for medevac operations
6. **Brief the inbound aircraft** when in contact
7. **Document everything** from the time of the hospital call

**Key principle:** Two hours is enough time to do this properly. A methodical reopening takes 20–30 minutes. Don't rush and create a new hazard.

</details>

## Module 7 — Norfolk Island specific

<details>
<summary>Scenario 15 — Sea fog developing with an inbound RPT 20 minutes out</summary>

It is 0630. You completed your opening inspection in clear conditions 30 minutes ago. Sea fog is beginning to form on the eastern horizon. The ATR-72 RPT service is 20 minutes out.

**Answer:**

1. **Take a current visibility observation** — estimate in metres from a fixed reference point
2. **Check the dewpoint spread** — if less than 2°C, the fog will develop quickly
3. **Contact the inbound aircraft immediately:**
   > *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, visibility currently [estimate], sea fog developing from the east, trend deteriorating. Wind [direction speed], QNH [value]. Advise your intentions."*
4. **Monitor every 2–3 minutes** and update the aircraft with each new observation
5. **Let the pilot decide** — your job is accurate real-time information. They decide whether to continue, hold, or divert
6. **Prepare for a possible diversion** — know the alternates, have contact numbers ready

**Key principle:** Norfolk Island sea fog can close the aerodrome in under five minutes. Give the pilot maximum warning and maximum accurate information. Early advice may allow them to expedite the approach safely.

</details>
