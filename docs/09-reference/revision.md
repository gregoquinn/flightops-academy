### Module 1 — Aviation fundamentals

<details>
<summary>Scenario 1 — A pilot asks about a taxiway closure you are not aware of</summary>

A pilot calls you on the CTAF and asks whether a particular section of taxiway is closed. You are not aware of a closure. Where do you check first, and what do you say to the pilot while you are checking?

**Answer**

Check NAIPS for active NOTAMs applicable to YSNF. While you are checking, tell the pilot:

> *"Stand by, confirming NOTAM status."*

Do not guess. If no NOTAM is active, the taxiway is not notamed closed — but visually confirm it is serviceable before saying so.

**Key principle:** Never give a pilot information you cannot verify.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 2 — A CASA officer arrives unannounced</summary>

A CASA officer arrives unannounced and asks to see your aerodrome manual. What do you do?

**Answer**

Provide it immediately. CASA has the right under the Civil Aviation Act 1988 to inspect documents at certified aerodromes. The aerodrome manual must be current and accessible at all times.

**Key principle:** Cooperate fully. Hesitation turns a routine visit into a compliance problem.

</details>

### Module 2 — Daily operations

<details>
<summary>Scenario 3 — Four consecutive runway edge lights unserviceable, first aircraft in 40 minutes</summary>

You are 20 minutes into your opening inspection when you find four consecutive runway edge lights unserviceable on the left side of runway 11. The first scheduled aircraft is inbound in 40 minutes. What do you do?

**Answer**

1. **Record the defect** — exact location, time, and nature
2. **Assess severity** — MOS Part 139 allows a maximum of two consecutive unserviceable lights. Four requires action
3. **Issue a NOTAM** — before the aircraft arrives, not after
4. **Contact maintenance** — arrange repair as a priority
5. **Advise the inbound aircraft** via broadcast or direct radio
6. **Document everything** in the shift log with times

**Key principle:** The NOTAM must be active before the aircraft arrives.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 4 — Unrecognised vehicle on the apron without a visible permit</summary>

During operations you observe a vehicle on the apron that you do not recognise and that does not appear to have an airside permit displayed. What do you do?

**Answer**

1. Approach the vehicle or contact its occupants immediately
2. Ask to see their **airside vehicle permit** and **ASIC**
3. If they cannot produce either, escort them to the terminal and record the incident
4. If they refuse or behave suspiciously, treat as a security incident
5. Document in the shift log

**Key principle:** Every vehicle on the movement area must be authorised.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 5 — Unserviceable lighting in the previous shift log but no NOTAM issued</summary>

You arrive for the morning shift and find the previous shift log shows runway edge lighting was unserviceable but no NOTAM was issued. The first aircraft is 25 minutes away.

**Answer**

1. **Issue the NOTAM immediately**
2. **Inspect the lights yourself** to confirm and assess whether it has worsened
3. **Contact maintenance** to arrange repair
4. **Advise the inbound aircraft** via broadcast
5. **Record in your shift log** that you found the omission and the action you took
6. **Report the omission** to your supervisor

**Key principle:** You are responsible for the shift you are on. Fix it, report it, move on.

</details>

### Module 3 — Aerodrome Reporting Officer

<details>
<summary>Scenario 6 — Possible tyre problem on landing, next aircraft 15 minutes out</summary>

An inbound ATR-72 reports a possible tyre problem on landing. The aircraft vacates normally and taxies to the apron. The next inbound aircraft is 15 minutes out.

**Answer**

1. **Broadcast immediately:**
   > *"All stations Norfolk, runway inspection in progress, runway temporarily unavailable, standby."*
2. **Conduct an immediate runway inspection** — look for tyre fragments, rubber debris, surface damage, and fluid trails
3. **If the runway is clear** — reopen and advise the inbound aircraft
4. **If debris is found** — clear it, complete the inspection, then reopen
5. **Notify the airline** — the aircraft may require a tyre inspection before next flight
6. **Document everything**

**Key principle:** Never let the next aircraft use the runway until you have physically inspected it.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 7 — Three Cape Pigeons on the runway, no movement for 45 minutes</summary>

During your runway inspection you find three Cape Pigeons feeding near the threshold 11 area. There are no movements for 45 minutes.

**Answer**

1. **Record the sighting** — species, number, location, time
2. **Attempt dispersal** using approved methods
3. **Monitor** — confirm birds have left
4. **Check again** closer to the movement time — birds return
5. **If birds cannot be dispersed** as the movement approaches, consider a short delay

**Key principle:** Dispersal is not a one-time action. A bird dispersed at 0545 may be back at 0600.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 8 — Unidentified metal fragment on the runway, 20 minutes to next movement</summary>

During your afternoon inspection you find a piece of metal approximately 5cm long on the runway centreline, 200m from threshold 29. The next movement is in 20 minutes.

**Answer**

1. **Pick it up** — place in your FOD bag
2. **Photograph it** before removal if possible
3. **Record the find** — time, location, description
4. **Continue the inspection** — one piece of FOD often means more nearby
5. **Investigate the source** — report to maintenance
6. **No NOTAM required** for FOD that has been removed and runway confirmed clear

**Key principle:** Remove it, record it, investigate the source.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 9 — Faded hold short marking on Taxiway Alpha, RPT arrival in 30 minutes</summary>

During your morning inspection you notice the hold short marking on Taxiway Alpha at the runway 11 intersection is significantly faded — visible but not clearly defined yellow. The first RPT arrival is in 30 minutes.

**Answer**

1. **Record the defect** — location, condition description, time
2. **Assess severity** — a hold short marking that is difficult to see is a significant safety defect. Any vehicle driver who cannot clearly see the marking may inadvertently cross onto the runway
3. **Notify maintenance** — arrange repainting as a priority
4. **Issue a NOTAM** if the condition is severe enough to affect operations
5. **Brief any airside vehicle operators** on shift of the reduced visibility of the marking — heightened caution required at that intersection
6. **Document in the shift log**

**Key principle:** Hold short markings are the last barrier before a runway incursion. Their serviceability is non-negotiable.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 10 — Situational awareness breakdown — multiple competing demands</summary>

You are conducting your opening runway inspection when your radio announces an inbound aircraft 20 miles out. At the same time, you find four consecutive edge lights unserviceable. Your phone rings — it is the contractor asking if they can bring their crane on site. What is your priority sequence and why?

**Answer**

**Priority sequence:**

1. **Radio — acknowledge the inbound aircraft** and advise the runway inspection is in progress:
   > *"All stations Norfolk, operations vehicle on runway 11, runway inspection in progress, standby."*
   This manages the immediate conflict between your vehicle and the inbound aircraft.

2. **Assess the lighting defect** — four consecutive lights is a NOTAM-level defect. Can the aircraft land safely in current conditions? If daytime VMC, the lights are not needed for this landing. Record the defect.

3. **Vacate the runway** and complete your broadcast before the aircraft reaches final approach.

4. **Issue the NOTAM** from the operations room for the unserviceable lights.

5. **Call the contractor back** — the crane question can wait until the immediate situation is managed. Do not allow crane operations to commence without OLS assessment regardless.

**Key principle:** Prioritise the aircraft safety conflict first, then manage the cascade. The phone rings last.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 11 — OLS penetration calculation — contractor stockpile near threshold</summary>

A contractor proposes positioning a material stockpile 80m from the threshold 29 end, 15m to the right of the runway centreline. The stockpile will be 4m high. Does it penetrate the OLS? What action is required?

**Answer**

**Calculation:**
- Surface: Approach surface (object is beyond the threshold)
- Slope: 2% (1:50)
- Distance from threshold: 80m
- Maximum permitted height: 80 × 0.02 = **1.6m AGL**
- Stockpile height: 4m
- Penetration: 4 - 1.6 = **2.4m penetration**

**Yes — the stockpile penetrates the OLS.**

**Required actions:**
1. The stockpile cannot be placed at this position at this height during runway operations
2. Options: move it further from the threshold, reduce height below 1.6m, or schedule placement during a runway closure
3. If a runway closure is used — issue a NOTAM for both the closure and the obstacle
4. If operations continue with reduced stockpile height — issue a NOTAM for the obstacle

**Key principle:** Calculate using the extended height and actual position. Do not assume proximity to the apron means clearance of the OLS.

</details>

### Module 4 — Works Safety Officer

<details>
<summary>Scenario 12 — Contractor wants to bring a 12-metre elevated work platform airside</summary>

A contractor asks to bring a 12-metre elevated work platform onto the apron to replace a floodlight. What do you do before authorising it airside?

**Answer**

1. **Get the extended height** in writing — not stowed height, extended boom height
2. **Plot the OLS** — does extended equipment at the working position penetrate any surface?
3. **If OLS penetration occurs** — assess whether operations can continue with a NOTAM, or schedule during a runway closure
4. **Issue a NOTAM** — location, maximum height, period of works
5. **Brief the contractor** — ASIC, vehicle permit, radio, speed limit, no independent movement
6. **Monitor throughout**

**Key principle:** OLS assessment before anything else. Stowed height is irrelevant — extended height at working position is what matters.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario 13 — Contractor working outside their approved area</summary>

You are supervising a maintenance crew replacing a taxiway edge light. You notice two workers have walked 80m further along the taxiway and are examining pavement not included in the approved works.

**Answer**

1. **Stop them immediately**
2. **Confirm the approved scope** — their authority covers the light replacement only
3. **Explain why** — the unapproved area is not covered by the works safety plan or NOTAM
4. **If they spotted a real defect** — they report it to you, you schedule it properly
5. **Document the incident** in the shift log
6. **Advise your supervisor**

**Key principle:** Scope creep is one of the most common ways airside safety controls are undermined.

</details>

### Module 5 — Radio communications

<details>
<summary>Scenario 14 — Pilot asks you to say again the runway in use</summary>

You are broadcasting on the CTAF and an aircraft responds: *"Norfolk Traffic, [callsign], say again runway in use."*

**Answer**

Respond clearly and completely:

> *"[Callsign], Norfolk Traffic, runway in use runway one one, wind zero niner zero at one two knots, QNH one zero one four."*

- Speak at a measured pace
- Say "one one" not "eleven"
- Give the full information block
- Do not apologise — just give the information

**Key principle:** "Say again" is a normal request, not a criticism.

</details>

### Module 6 — Emergency management

<details>
<summary>Scenario 15 — Mayday declared, engine failure on approach, you are alone on shift</summary>

An aircraft transmits: *"Mayday, Mayday, Mayday, [callsign], engine failure, continuing approach."* You are the only person on shift.

**Answer**

In this exact order:

1. **Acknowledge:**
   > *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, Mayday acknowledged, runway 11 clear, emergency services being activated."*
2. **Activate emergency services** — call the fire service
3. **Clear the runway** — confirm no other aircraft or vehicles present
4. **Maintain radio contact** — ask for souls on board and fuel
5. **Notify the airline**
6. **Document** — start recording times and actions

**Key principle:** Acknowledge first, activate services second. The priority order saves lives.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 16 — Nose gear collapsed on runway, fire services on scene</summary>

After a declared emergency landing, the aircraft is on the runway with the nose gear collapsed. Fire services are on scene. No other movements for two hours.

**Answer**

1. **Confirm fire risk is managed**
2. **Notify supervisor and aerodrome operator**
3. **Issue a NOTAM** — runway closed, disabled aircraft on runway
4. **Notify Brisbane Centre**
5. **Notify the ATSB** — 1800 011 034. Do this early
6. **Coordinate aircraft recovery**
7. **Preserve the scene** — no unauthorised access until ATSB authorises
8. **Document continuously**

**Key principle:** ATSB notification is mandatory and time-sensitive. Call them early.

</details>

---

<details>
<summary>Scenario 17 — Medevac request after the aerodrome has closed for the night</summary>

You have completed the closing inspection when the Norfolk Island Hospital calls. A patient requires urgent medevac. A medevac aircraft is two hours away.

**Answer**

1. **Confirm details** — patient condition, medical staff, aircraft type, ETA
2. **Contact the airline medevac coordination desk**
3. **Reopen the aerodrome** — activate lighting, open operations room, conduct runway inspection
4. **Coordinate ambulance access**
5. **Notify Brisbane Centre**
6. **Brief the inbound aircraft** when in radio contact
7. **Document everything** from the time of the hospital call

**Key principle:** Two hours is enough time to do this properly.

</details>

### Module 7 — Norfolk Island specific

<details>
<summary>Scenario 18 — Sea fog developing with an inbound RPT 20 minutes out</summary>

It is 0630. Sea fog is beginning to form on the eastern horizon. The ATR-72 RPT service is 20 minutes out. Dewpoint spread is 1.5°C.

**Answer**

1. **Take a visibility observation** — estimate in metres
2. **Contact the inbound aircraft immediately:**
   > *"[Callsign], Norfolk Radio, visibility currently [estimate], sea fog developing from the east, trend deteriorating. Wind [direction speed], QNH [value]. Advise your intentions."*
3. **Update every 2–3 minutes** with fresh observations
4. **Let the pilot decide** — provide accurate real-time information
5. **Prepare for a possible diversion** — know the alternates, have contact numbers ready

**Key principle:** Sea fog at YSNF can close the aerodrome in under five minutes. Give maximum early warning.

</details>
