# Contractor management

Every person who enters the movement area of a certified aerodrome must be briefed, supervised, and controlled. Contractors — maintenance crews, construction workers, fuel technicians, cleaning crews — often have no aviation background and no instinct for the unique hazards of an airside environment. Your briefing and supervision may be the only thing standing between them and a fatal accident.

---

## The fundamental principle

On the airside, you are in charge. Not the contractor's supervisor. Not the project manager. Not the airline operations manager. You, the FOO or WSO, have authority over the movement area. A contractor who refuses to follow your instructions must leave the airside immediately.

This is not about status. It is about the fact that you are the person with the regulatory responsibility for safe operations on that movement area.

---

## ASIC requirements

Every person who enters a security-controlled area of a certified aerodrome must hold a valid **Aviation Security Identification Card (ASIC)** or be under the continuous escort of an ASIC holder.

Before any contractor enters the airside area:
- Check their ASIC is current and displayed
- Check the ASIC allows access to the areas they will be working in
- If they do not hold an ASIC, they must be escorted by an ASIC holder at all times

> ⚠️ **Important:** "Continuous escort" means exactly that — the ASIC holder must remain with the non-ASIC holder at all times. A contractor cannot be left alone airside because their escort was briefly called away.

---

## Airside vehicle permits

Any vehicle operating on the movement area must have a current airside vehicle permit. The permit is issued by the airport operator and confirms the vehicle has been inspected and is appropriate for airside use.

Before allowing a contractor's vehicle airside:
- Confirm the vehicle has a current permit displayed
- If the vehicle is new to the aerodrome, conduct a vehicle inspection before issuing a permit
- Ensure the vehicle is fitted with an amber flashing beacon
- Ensure a radio is installed and operational (or a handheld radio is provided)

## The pre-works briefing

Every contractor must receive a briefing before entering the movement area for the first time on each job. Do not assume contractors remember last week's briefing. A fresh job gets a fresh briefing.

### Briefing content

**Who you are and your authority**
Introduce yourself. Explain that as the WSO/FOO you are responsible for the safety of the movement area and that your instructions must be followed.

**The specific work today**
Confirm your understanding of what the work involves matches the contractor's understanding. If there is a discrepancy, resolve it before anyone enters the airside.

**The work area boundaries**
Show the contractor exactly where they are permitted to work. Walk the boundaries if necessary. Be explicit about what is inside and outside the permitted area.

**Movement restrictions**
- No vehicle moves on the movement area without your explicit authorisation
- When an aircraft is approaching, landing, or taxiing, all vehicles stop and hold clear
- No vehicle crosses a runway or active taxiway without explicit clearance from you
- Speed limits apply at all times (typically 25 km/h or as per the aerodrome manual)

**Radio procedures**
- The required radio frequency for the work period
- The callsign for their vehicle
- What to say when requesting movement
- What to do if the radio fails

**Emergency procedures**
- What to do if they see an aircraft approaching unexpectedly
- Where the emergency assembly point is
- Who to call in an emergency and how

**Wildlife**
- Do not disturb nesting wildlife without FOO instruction
- Report any wildlife on the movement area immediately
- Do not feed birds or leave food scraps airside

**FOD**
- Account for all tools and materials at all times
- No loose items outside secured containers
- Report any dropped tools or materials immediately
- Conduct a tool and material check before leaving the worksite

**Personal safety**
- High-visibility vest must be worn at all times
- Eye and ear protection when required by the work activity
- No smoking airside except in designated areas
- Do not walk behind operating aircraft engines or propellers

### Briefing documentation

Record the briefing in the shift log. Note:
- Date and time of briefing
- Names of contractors briefed
- Company
- Nature of works
- Any specific conditions

Have the lead contractor sign the briefing record if your aerodrome manual requires it.

## Supervising contractors during works

Supervision does not mean standing and watching every action. It means maintaining awareness of what is happening and being available to intervene immediately if something is going wrong.

**Maintain radio contact.** Contractors should check in by radio if they are moving between areas or beginning a new activity. You should be able to reach any works vehicle at any time.

**Make regular visual checks.** If you are not present at the worksite continuously, make regular visits — particularly before any aircraft movement.

**Watch for scope creep.** Contractors sometimes expand their work beyond what was planned. A maintenance crew fixing a light might decide to also check the adjacent conduit run — and suddenly they are somewhere they are not supposed to be. Watch for this and redirect immediately.

**Aircraft movements take priority.** Every time an aircraft moves, verify all contractors are holding their agreed position. Do not assume — confirm by radio or visual check.

---

## Vehicle escorts

Some situations require a physical vehicle escort — a works vehicle follows the escort vehicle (your operations vehicle) into the movement area and back out. Escort is required when:

- The contractor is not familiar with the aerodrome layout
- The work area requires crossing an active runway or taxiway
- The contractor's vehicle is large and the driver has limited visibility

During an escort:
- Lead from the front at all times
- Maintain radio contact with the escorted vehicle
- Move at a pace the escort can match
- Do not enter an active runway or taxiway without conducting a radio broadcast and visual check

---

## When contractors do not comply

If a contractor fails to follow your instructions, the sequence is:

1. **Stop the work.** Clearly instruct the contractor to stop what they are doing.
2. **Correct the behaviour.** Explain what they have done wrong and what they need to do.
3. **If they comply,** document the incident and continue with heightened monitoring.
4. **If they refuse to comply,** direct them to leave the airside area. Notify your supervisor immediately. Do not allow them to return until the issue is resolved.

Document every non-compliance incident in the shift log, regardless of how minor. A pattern of non-compliance from a contractor company is a safety management issue that must be escalated.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** CASR Part 139.090 places the obligation for safe aerodrome operations on the aerodrome operator. As the WSO, you carry out that obligation on their behalf. Non-compliant contractors who cause incidents can result in regulatory action against both the contractor and the aerodrome operator.
