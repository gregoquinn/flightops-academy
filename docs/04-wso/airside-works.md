# Airside works planning

Works on or near the movement area of a certified aerodrome are among the highest-risk activities an airport manages. A construction vehicle in the wrong place at the wrong moment can cause a fatal accident. As a Works Safety Officer, you are the person responsible for ensuring that does not happen.

---

## Regulatory basis

> 📋 **CASA reference:** CASR Part 139, Subpart E — Aerodrome works safety. MOS Part 139, Chapter 5 — Aerodrome works safety. CASA Advisory Circular AC 139-08 — Aerodrome works safety.

The MOS and AC 139-08 are your primary references for planning and managing airside works. Read them before your first works job, not during it.

---

## What constitutes airside works

Airside works includes any work that:

- Takes place on the movement area (runway, taxiway, apron)
- Takes place adjacent to the movement area within the runway strip or OLS
- Introduces objects or vehicles that could constitute an obstacle to aircraft operations
- Involves excavation, construction, or installation near aircraft operations areas
- Could affect aerodrome lighting, markings, or navigation aids

This includes everything from repainting a taxiway line to resurfacing a runway, from replacing a light fitting to installing new fencing.

---

## The WSO's responsibilities

As the Works Safety Officer, you are responsible for:

1. **Planning** — assessing the work, identifying hazards, and developing a works safety plan
2. **Authorisation** — confirming the work is approved and that the appropriate NOTAMs have been issued
3. **Briefing** — briefing all contractors and workers before they enter the movement area
4. **Supervision** — monitoring the work while it is in progress
5. **Coordination** — managing the interface between the work and aircraft operations
6. **Closure** — confirming the area is clear and safe when work is complete

## Planning airside works — the sequence

### Step 1 — Understand the scope

Before any planning begins, understand exactly what the work involves:
- What is being done?
- Where exactly?
- What equipment will be used?
- How many workers?
- How long will it take?
- Will operations need to be suspended?

Get this information from the person ordering the work (maintenance manager, airport manager, or contractor) in writing.

### Step 2 — Assess the hazards

Work through the following questions:

**OLS penetration**
Will any equipment (cranes, elevated work platforms, trucks, stockpiles) penetrate the Obstacle Limitation Surface? Calculate the maximum height of equipment and plot it against the OLS for its proposed location. If an OLS penetration occurs, a NOTAM is required. Some OLS penetrations require a runway closure.

**Proximity to the movement area**
How close is the work to the active runway or taxiway? The closer the work, the higher the risk. Work on or immediately adjacent to the runway requires the most rigorous controls.

**Time and traffic**
Can the work be scheduled during a period of low or no traffic? Night works or works during scheduled gaps in RPT operations reduce the risk of conflict between aircraft and vehicles.

**Lighting and marking impacts**
Will the work affect any lights or markings? If temporary lighting deactivation is required, what alternative arrangements are needed?

**Access and egress**
How will vehicles and workers enter and exit the work area? What is the risk of inadvertent runway incursion?

### Step 3 — Develop the works safety plan

For any significant airside works, a written works safety plan should be prepared. This does not need to be a lengthy document, but it must cover:

- Description of the work
- Location (with diagram or photograph)
- Period of work
- Equipment to be used and maximum heights
- OLS assessment
- Any required runway or taxiway closures
- NOTAM requirements
- Communication plan (radio frequencies, contact numbers)
- Emergency procedures
- Contractor briefing requirements
- Site-specific safety rules

For minor works (e.g., replacing a single light fitting) a simplified plan or checklist is sufficient.

### Step 4 — Obtain approval

Before any works commence, the works safety plan must be approved by the aerodrome operator (or their delegate). For significant works affecting operations, notify Airservices Australia if the work will generate NOTAMs.

### Step 5 — Issue NOTAMs

Issue all required NOTAMs before works commence. The NOTAM must be active before any equipment enters the movement area. See the NOTAM creation chapter for content and procedures.

## Worksite establishment

Once planning is complete and approvals are obtained, the worksite can be established.

### Marking the worksite boundary

The boundary between the work area and the operational area must be clearly marked. Approved marking methods include:

- **Cones** (orange, minimum 500mm height) for temporary boundaries
- **Barriers** for longer-term or higher-risk delineation
- **Flashing amber lights** for visibility at night or in reduced visibility
- **Red obstacle lights** on any equipment that may penetrate the OLS

The marking must be sufficient to prevent any inadvertent entry by aircraft or vehicles into the worksite, and prevent any inadvertent entry by worksite vehicles onto the operational area.

### Closing and reopening sections

If a section of taxiway, runway, or apron is to be closed:

- Mark the closure clearly with cones, barriers, and where appropriate, Xs on the pavement
- Ensure the closure is reflected in the active NOTAM before any aircraft movement
- When reopening after works are complete, conduct a thorough inspection of the area before removing the closure markings and cancelling the NOTAM

## During works — your role

While works are in progress, the WSO must:

- **Be present** or have a designated representative who is present and contactable at all times
- **Monitor radio** — all works vehicles operating near the movement area must carry an operational radio on the CTAF frequency (or as specified in the works safety plan)
- **Control vehicle movements** — no vehicle enters or exits the worksite without your authority
- **Maintain situational awareness** — know where every aircraft and every vehicle is at all times
- **Be prepared to suspend works** — if an unplanned aircraft arrival occurs, or conditions change, you must be able to suspend works and ensure all equipment is clear of the movement area immediately

> ⚠️ **Important:** No contractor has the authority to move a vehicle onto the operational area without your direct authorisation. Make this clear in the pre-works briefing. The contractor may be experienced at construction — they are not experienced at aerodrome safety unless they have aerodrome-specific training.

---

## Suspending works for aircraft movements

The most critical moment in any airside works operation is when an aircraft needs to use the movement area while works are in progress.

**Standard procedure:**

1. Receive notification of inbound aircraft (from radio monitoring or schedule)
2. Issue a suspension order to all works vehicles: *"All works vehicles, suspend operations, hold position."*
3. Confirm all vehicles are stopped and clear of the operational area
4. Conduct a visual check — can you confirm no equipment is on or near the runway or taxiway to be used?
5. Advise the aircraft (or broadcast) that the runway is clear
6. Monitor the aircraft throughout the approach and landing
7. Once the aircraft has vacated, confirm it is clear and resume works

> 💡 **Operational tip:** Do not rush the resumption of works. Wait until the aircraft is fully clear of the area and you have visually confirmed its position before allowing vehicles to move again.
