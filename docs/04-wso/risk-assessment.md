# Risk assessment

Risk assessment is the process of identifying hazards, analysing their likelihood and consequence, and implementing controls to reduce risk to an acceptable level. It underpins every decision you make as a WSO — from deciding whether a works plan is safe, to judging whether a defect requires an immediate NOTAM or can wait for the next scheduled inspection.

---

## The risk management framework

Australia's aviation system uses a risk management approach consistent with ICAO's Safety Management Systems (SMS) framework. At the aerodrome level, this means:

1. **Identify** hazards — what could go wrong?
2. **Analyse** risk — how likely, and how severe?
3. **Control** the risk — what can we do to reduce it?
4. **Monitor** — is the control working?

---

## The risk matrix

The standard tool for risk assessment is the **risk matrix**. It combines two factors:

**Likelihood** — how probable is it that the hazard will lead to an incident?

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| 5 — Almost certain | Expected to occur in most circumstances |
| 4 — Likely | Will probably occur in most circumstances |
| 3 — Possible | Might occur at some time |
| 2 — Unlikely | Could occur at some time |
| 1 — Rare | May occur only in exceptional circumstances |

**Consequence** — if the hazard leads to an incident, how bad would it be?

| Level | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 5 — Catastrophic | Multiple fatalities, aircraft destroyed | Aircraft crash with fatalities |
| 4 — Major | Single fatality or serious injuries | Serious aircraft damage, major injury |
| 3 — Moderate | Significant injuries, significant damage | Tyre burst, vehicle collision |
| 2 — Minor | Minor injury, minor damage | Scratched aircraft, personnel cut |
| 1 — Negligible | No injury, no significant damage | Near-miss with no contact |

**Risk rating = Likelihood × Consequence**

| Rating | Risk level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 15–25 | Extreme | Do not proceed. Immediate action required. |
| 8–14 | High | Senior management approval required before proceeding. Significant controls needed. |
| 4–7 | Medium | Manage with documented controls. Monitor closely. |
| 1–3 | Low | Manage with routine procedures. |

## Applying risk assessment to airside works

Before any significant airside works begin, a formal risk assessment should be documented. The following worked example demonstrates the process.

### Example: Runway reseal — one half-day runway closure

**Step 1 — Identify hazards**

| Hazard | What could go wrong? |
|---|---|
| Works vehicle on runway during operations | Aircraft lands on runway occupied by vehicle — collision |
| OLS penetration by crane | Aircraft on approach strikes crane |
| Sealing compound on taxiway | Aircraft slides on wet sealant during taxi |
| Workers on runway | Worker struck by aircraft or vehicle |
| Inadequate NOTAM | Pilot unaware of closure, attempts to land |
| Communications failure | WSO cannot contact works vehicle when aircraft approaches |

**Step 2 — Analyse risk (before controls)**

| Hazard | Likelihood | Consequence | Rating | Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vehicle/aircraft collision on runway | 3 | 5 | 15 | Extreme |
| Crane penetrating OLS | 2 | 5 | 10 | High |
| Wet sealant on taxiway | 3 | 3 | 9 | High |
| Worker struck on runway | 2 | 5 | 10 | High |
| Inadequate NOTAM | 2 | 5 | 10 | High |
| Communications failure | 2 | 5 | 10 | High |

**Step 3 — Implement controls**

| Hazard | Controls |
|---|---|
| Vehicle/aircraft collision | Runway closed to aircraft. NOTAM issued. Works halt for any unplanned traffic. WSO present during all works. |
| Crane penetrating OLS | Crane positioned to remain below OLS. NOTAM issued for obstacle. Crane height confirmed by supplier. |
| Wet sealant on taxiway | Taxiway barrier erected at sealant boundary. Works complete before taxiway reopened. |
| Worker struck on runway | Works only when runway closed. Hi-vis vests. No worker in movement area during any aircraft movement. |
| Inadequate NOTAM | NOTAM filed 24 hours in advance. NOTAM confirmed active before works start. Airline notified directly. |
| Communications failure | Two radios on worksite. Handheld backup. Agreed visual signal for radio failure. |

**Step 4 — Re-analyse risk (with controls)**

| Hazard | Residual Likelihood | Residual Consequence | Residual Rating | Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vehicle/aircraft collision | 1 | 5 | 5 | Medium |
| Crane penetrating OLS | 1 | 5 | 5 | Medium |
| Wet sealant on taxiway | 1 | 3 | 3 | Low |
| Worker struck on runway | 1 | 5 | 5 | Medium |
| Inadequate NOTAM | 1 | 5 | 5 | Medium |
| Communications failure | 1 | 5 | 5 | Medium |

All hazards are now at Medium or Low — acceptable to proceed with the planned controls in place.

## Risk assessment for operational decisions

You do not always have time to complete a formal written risk assessment. In routine operational decisions, the same thinking process applies — it is just faster and often mental.

**Example — Operational decision:**
Three minutes before an inbound ATR-72 lands, you spot a flock of 12 starlings on the runway centreline near the threshold.

Rapid mental risk assessment:
- Hazard: Bird strike on landing roll
- Likelihood: High (birds on the runway, aircraft about to land)
- Consequence: Major (engine ingestion possible, tyre burst possible)
- Rating: High
- Control options: Alert the pilot, advise them to circle while you attempt dispersal

Decision: Broadcast wildlife warning, advise pilot, attempt dispersal before landing if time permits. If birds cannot be dispersed in time, ensure the pilot is informed and let them make the landing decision with full information.

## Documenting risk assessments

For planned works, risk assessments must be documented. The documentation does not need to be complex, but it must show:

- The hazards identified
- The initial risk rating
- The controls implemented
- The residual risk rating
- Who conducted the assessment and when

File the risk assessment with the works safety plan. It demonstrates that the works were planned safely and provides evidence of due diligence if an incident occurs.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** MOS Part 139, Chapter 5 requires a safety assessment for works on or near the movement area. AC 139-08 provides guidance on the content of that assessment.

## Safety management system

At a larger aerodrome, the risk assessment process is embedded in a formal Safety Management System (SMS). On Norfolk Island, the scale is smaller, but the same principles apply. Your inspection records, defect reports, NOTAM filings, and incident reports are all part of the aerodrome's safety record.

A good safety culture means:
- Hazards are reported, not hidden
- Near-misses are recorded and analysed
- Lessons from incidents are shared
- Procedures are reviewed and improved

As a FOO/WSO, you are a key part of that culture, regardless of the size of the aerodrome.
