# Shift overview

A shift at a certified aerodrome follows a consistent rhythm. While no two shifts are identical — weather, traffic, wildlife, and unexpected events all vary — the underlying structure of the day is the same. This chapter describes that structure so you understand the framework before you study each individual component in detail.

---

## The three phases of a shift

Every shift can be broken into three phases:

```
[OPENING]         [OPERATIONS]         [CLOSING]
 
Inspection    →   Aircraft movements →  Final inspection
Activation        Coordination          Documentation
Briefing          Monitoring            Handover
```

---

## Opening phase

The opening phase begins before the first aircraft movement of the day. It typically starts 30–60 minutes before the first scheduled arrival.

**1. Shift handover (if relieving)**
If you are relieving another operator, read the shift log from the previous period. Note any outstanding defects, NOTAMs, or issues that carry over. Verbal handover follows written — ask questions.

**2. Weather check**
Review the current METAR, TAF, and any SIGMETs or AIRMETs applicable to the aerodrome. Note wind direction and strength — this determines runway in use. Check for any forecast conditions that may affect operations (low visibility, crosswinds, thunderstorms).

**3. NOTAM review**
Check NAIPS for all NOTAMs applicable to your aerodrome. Note any that are currently active or will become active during your shift. Brief any relevant NOTAMs to aircraft on first contact or via broadcast.

**4. Movement area inspection**
Conduct the opening runway inspection in accordance with the aerodrome manual. This includes runway, taxiways, aprons, and associated lighting and markings. The inspection is documented on the runway inspection form.

**5. Lighting check**
Check all aerodrome lighting systems — runway edge lights, threshold lights, taxiway lights, approach lights, and the aerodrome beacon. Record any unserviceable lights.

**6. Wildlife inspection**
Conduct or confirm a wildlife check of the movement area and immediate surrounds. Record species, numbers, and location. Disperse if required.

**7. Open the aerodrome**
Once the inspection is complete and the aerodrome is confirmed serviceable, activate the appropriate systems (lighting, ATIS if applicable, radio monitoring) and record the time of opening in the shift log.

---

## Operations phase

During the operations phase, your role shifts from active inspection to monitoring, coordination, and response.

**Continuous monitoring**
- Monitor the CTAF/MBZ for inbound traffic calls
- Monitor weather — re-check METAR each hour and whenever conditions change
- Monitor the movement area visually and via CCTV where available

**Aircraft arrivals**
- Note ETA from pilot broadcast or airline schedule
- Confirm runway in use is clear and serviceable
- Provide aerodrome information on request or via broadcast
- Observe landing and note any anomalies (heavy landing, runway excursion, smoke)
- Inspect runway after heavy or abnormal landings if required

**Aircraft departures**
- Confirm aircraft intent (destination, fuel requirements, cargo)
- Provide current wind, QNH, and runway in use
- Monitor departure roll and initial climb

**RPT coordination**
For regular public transport (RPT) operations, there is typically a coordinated sequence involving the airline ground handling agent, fuel supplier, baggage handlers, and customs/biosecurity. Your role is to ensure aerodrome safety is maintained throughout — including keeping the apron clear of unauthorised personnel.

**Defect management**
If a defect is identified during operations (lighting failure, pavement damage, FOD), assess its severity, act to make it safe, and issue a NOTAM if it affects operations. Record it in the shift log.

---

## Closing phase

The closing phase begins after the last aircraft movement of the day.

**1. Final inspection**
Conduct a closing inspection of the movement area. This is the same format as the opening inspection. Note any new defects.

**2. Lighting**
Ensure all lighting is set correctly for the closed period (typically pilot-activated or minimum setting, per aerodrome manual).

**3. Security**
Confirm all access gates are locked and the movement area is secured.

**4. Documentation**
Complete the daily shift log. Ensure all movements, inspections, defects, NOTAMs, and incidents are recorded accurately. The shift log is a legal record.

**5. Handover**
If you are being relieved, brief the incoming operator verbally and ensure they have read the shift log. If you are the last person closing, follow the aerodrome manual closing procedure.

---

## Shift log entries — what to record

Every entry in the shift log should include:
- **Time** (UTC or local, consistent throughout)
- **What happened**
- **What action was taken**
- **Outcome**

> 💡 **Operational tip:** Write your shift log entries as if a CASA auditor will read them tomorrow — because they might. Clear, factual, timestamped entries are evidence of professional operation. Gaps, vague entries, or missing records raise questions.

---

## Routine vs non-routine

The mark of a competent FOO is the ability to recognise when a situation has moved from routine to non-routine and to respond appropriately. Most shifts are routine. But the purpose of all the training, inspections, and procedures is to prepare you for the ones that are not.
