# RPT and charter operations

Regular Public Transport (RPT) operations are the heartbeat of Norfolk Island Airport. Every RPT movement involves a coordinated sequence of activities between the airline, ground handlers, fuel supplier, biosecurity, customs, and your operations team. Your role as FOO is to ensure the aerodrome side of that sequence runs safely.

---

## Understanding RPT operations

RPT means any scheduled air service carrying passengers or cargo for hire or reward. On Norfolk Island, RPT services are operated by regional airlines using turboprop or small jet aircraft — typically the ATR 72-500/600.

RPT operations are more demanding than GA or charter operations because:

- They operate to published schedules — delays have flow-on effects
- They carry passengers who may be unfamiliar with the aerodrome environment
- They involve multiple ground handling agencies working simultaneously on and around the aircraft
- They are subject to stricter regulatory oversight

---

## The arrival sequence

### 30 minutes before ETA

- Confirm ETA with the airline or ground handling agent
- Check weather — if conditions are deteriorating, advise the airline
- Confirm runway in use and that it is clear and serviceable
- Ensure apron bay is clear and clean
- Confirm fuel order is ready if refuelling is planned
- Ensure ground handling equipment is positioned but clear of aircraft paths
- Confirm biosecurity and customs are notified (for international or regional international services)

### Aircraft on approach

- Monitor CTAF for the aircraft's approach calls
- Conduct a final visual check of the runway threshold area
- Be ready to broadcast any last-minute information (wind shift, wildlife on runway)
- If there is any reason the runway is not available, broadcast immediately

Standard arrival information broadcast (if not already provided):

> *"[Callsign], Norfolk, runway [number] clear, wind [direction] [speed] knots, QNH [value], [any relevant NOTAMs or information]."*

### After landing

- Monitor the aircraft vacating the runway
- If the aircraft reports any abnormality (hard landing, possible tyre issue, bird strike), initiate post-landing inspection before the next movement
- Ensure apron marshalling is conducted safely if a marshaller is used
- Record landing time in the shift log

## The turnaround sequence

A turnaround is the period between an aircraft arriving and departing. On a small island with limited resources, the turnaround involves more manual processes than at a major airport.

### Typical turnaround elements (approximate sequence)

| Activity | Who | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Aircraft parks on bay | Pilot / marshaller | Chocks in when stopped |
| Passenger disembarkation | Airline / handler | Sterile corridor to terminal |
| Baggage offload | Handler | Manual process — allow time |
| Cargo offload | Handler | Check cargo manifest |
| Catering / cleaning | Contractor | Access requires ASIC |
| Refuelling | Fuel contractor | FOO monitors fuel safety |
| Baggage load (departing) | Handler | Weight and balance confirmed by airline |
| Passenger boarding | Airline | Head count against manifest |
| Doors closed | Airline | Notify FOO when complete |
| Pushback / start | Pilot | Confirm clear to start if required |
| Taxi | Pilot | Provide current wind and runway |

**Your role during the turnaround:**
- Ensure all personnel on the apron hold valid ASICs
- Ensure no unauthorised vehicles are within the safety zone of the aircraft
- Monitor refuelling for safety compliance
- Be available for radio contact at all times
- Be the single point of coordination if anything goes wrong

## Apron safety during turnarounds

The apron during a turnaround is the highest-risk environment at the aerodrome. People and vehicles are moving in close proximity to aircraft. Key safety rules:

**Safety zones**
No vehicles or personnel enter the aircraft safety zone (the area beneath the wing and fuselage arc) without the direct supervision of a qualified ground handler.

**Ground equipment**
All ground support equipment (GPU, stairs, baggage carts, fuel bowser) must approach and depart the aircraft from approved directions. High-speed approaches and crossing behind running engines are prohibited.

**Jet blast / prop wash**
The ATR 72 generates significant prop wash during engine start and taxi. Keep all personnel and unsecured equipment clear. Brief any visitors or contractors before engine start.

**Refuelling safety**
During refuelling:
- No smoking within 15 metres
- No engine starts
- Bonding lead connected before fuel cap opened
- Fire extinguisher accessible
- FOO or delegate monitors until complete

> ⚠️ **Important:** If a fuel spill occurs during refuelling, stop the fuelling immediately. Ensure ignition sources are removed. Contain the spill if safe to do so. Notify fire services. Issue a NOTAM if the spill affects the movement area.

## Charter and general aviation operations

Charter and GA operations are less structured than RPT but require the same safety standards.

**Typical differences from RPT:**
- Less predictable scheduling — ETA may change with short notice
- Smaller aircraft with different performance characteristics
- Pilots may be unfamiliar with Norfolk Island
- No dedicated ground handler — you may need to assist with parking, chocking, and fuel coordination
- Passengers may be more likely to wander — supervise access to the apron

**First-time visitors to YSNF**
Brief pilots unfamiliar with Norfolk Island on:
- Runway in use and any limitations
- Backtrack requirement (no parallel taxiway for full runway length)
- Fuel availability and contact for fuel
- Wildlife hazard — particularly birds in the early morning
- Local weather patterns including sea fog risk
- Emergency contacts

## Medical evacuation (medevac) operations

Medevac flights can be some of the most demanding operations you will manage, because:

- They often occur outside normal hours
- They involve stressed families and medical staff with no aerodrome experience
- The patient's condition may be serious and time-critical
- Night operations on a remote island with limited support add complexity

### Medevac activation sequence

1. Receive notification — from hospital, airline coordination, or directly from pilot
2. Open the aerodrome if outside normal hours (lighting, operations room)
3. Confirm aircraft type, ETA, and any special requirements (ambulance access, medical equipment clearance, customs/biosecurity if applicable)
4. Coordinate ambulance access to the apron — gate access, escort, positioning
5. Clear the apron bay
6. Brief the inbound pilot on current conditions
7. Ensure smooth, unhurried coordination on the ground
8. Document all times and actions

> 💡 **Operational tip:** During a medevac, the medical team will be focused on the patient, not on aerodrome safety. You are responsible for keeping them safe on the apron. Brief them firmly but calmly on where to stand and what not to touch before the aircraft arrives.

## Military and government operations

Occasional military or government aircraft will operate through YSNF. These may include:

- RAAF or Royal Australian Navy patrol aircraft
- Australian Border Force aircraft
- Medical charter for government personnel

Military operations may involve additional coordination requirements including security clearances, fuel arrangements, and restricted information about the mission. Follow your aerodrome manual and supervisor guidance for handling these visits.

> 📋 **CASA reference:** All aircraft operating in Australian airspace, including military aircraft, are subject to aerodrome safety standards when operating at certified aerodromes. The FOO's safety responsibilities do not change based on the aircraft type.
